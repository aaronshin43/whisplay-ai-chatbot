## Circulation Skill Station: External Bleeding Control

### Direct Pressure for External Bleeding
A wound that is deep and bleeding heavily may not stop on its own. Apply direct pressure:
1. Put on gloves.
2. Use gauze or another clean non-adherent dressing. Do NOT use bulky dressings.
3. Apply firm pressure as directly as possible to the source of bleeding, usually with two or three fingers.
4. If the first dressing becomes soaked with blood, do NOT remove — this will dislodge clots. Add another pad and apply firm pressure.
5. When bleeding stops, apply a bandage to keep the gauze/pad in place.
6. If bleeding does not stop, consider deep wound packing or tourniquet.

> **Note:** Limb elevation is NOT a primary hemorrhage control measure. Maintain direct pressure as the priority. Do not interrupt or delay pressure to position the limb.

### Deep Wound Packing for External Bleeding
If the wound is deep or gaping and simple pressure does not stop the bleeding:
1. Put on gloves.
2. Always assess pulses, capillary refill and sensation before and after dressing any wound.
3. Thoroughly wash out the wound by flushing with at least 1 litre of clean water (under pressure when possible).
4. Use gauze or another clean, compact material to completely fill the space within the wound.
5. Use additional gauze on top of the wound surface and apply direct pressure with gloved hand or a bandage wrapped firmly around the wound/limb.
6. For limb wounds requiring packing, apply a splint to reduce risk of re-bleeding.
7. A deep wound pack should NOT be left in place for more than 24 hours — risk of infection.
8. If bleeding does not stop, consider tourniquet.

### Tourniquet Technique for Uncontrolled External Bleeding
Use ONLY if all other bleeding control measures have failed AND haemorrhage is life-threatening. Placing a tourniquet may cause permanent tissue damage or require amputation. **CALL FOR HELP IMMEDIATELY** and plan for handover/transfer to surgical unit.

1. If available, use a pneumatic tourniquet (like a blood pressure cuff) over padded skin; inflate until bleeding stops. If not available, use a thick band, cloth, or belt (wider is better) over padded skin.
2. Apply as close to the wound as possible, but NOT over a wound or fracture.
3. Apply enough pressure to make distal pulses disappear. Reassess bleeding.
4. If bleeding stops, dress the wound and prepare for transfer to surgical care.
5. If bleeding does not stop, increase tourniquet pressure until major bleeding ceases.
6. **Record the exact time the tourniquet was applied** — write it on the patient's skin or the tourniquet itself.
7. Consult advanced provider as soon as possible (never more than 2 hours) after placing tourniquet.

⚠️ **PROTOCOL NOTE (WHO BEC 2018):** The WHO BEC text states the tourniquet should be released every 2 hours. However, current TCCC/MARCH protocol states: do NOT release or loosen a tourniquet in the pre-hospital setting. Transport to definitive surgical care with tourniquet in place, time of application marked on skin. Releasing in the field risks fatal re-bleeding.

- Tourniquet location: extremities ONLY; place above the level of bleeding. Upper arm or leg more effective than below elbow or knee.
- Make sure tourniquet is clearly visible.
- Tourniquet is a last resort — cutting off blood supply to the limb.

## Circulation Skill Station: Circulation Exam

- Check for anxiety, confusion, or altered mental status.
- Feel for pulse: rate and quality (normal adult: 60–100 beats/min).
- Assess capillary refill: push on fingernail, palm, or sole; normal return < 3 seconds.
- Assess skin colour and temperature.
- Measure vital signs: respiratory rate and blood pressure (normal adult: RR 10–20/min, systolic BP > 90 mmHg).

## Skill Assessment: Bleeding Control

**Skill 1 — Assess circulation:** pulse, capillary refill, skin colour/temperature, vital signs

**Skill 2 — Direct pressure:**
- Gloves on
- Gauze or clean non-adherent dressing; firm pressure; do not use bulky dressings
- Do NOT remove first dressing; add second pad on top
- Apply bandage when bleeding ceases
- Call for help
- **Do NOT** interrupt pressure to elevate limb — direct pressure is the priority

**Skill 3 — Deep wound packing:**
- Gloves on
- Assess pulses, capillary refill, sensation before and after
- Irrigate with 1L clean water before packing
- Pack wound completely; apply pressure with hand or firm bandage
- Splint limb wound to reduce re-bleeding risk
- Pack should not remain > 24 hours (infection risk)

**Skill 4 — Tourniquet:**
- Indication: continued uncontrolled life-threatening bleeding after all other measures failed
- Blood pressure cuff or thick band/cloth/belt over padded skin
- Tighten until distal pulses disappear and bleeding stops
- Secure in place; document time of application
- Plan for handover/transfer to surgical care; do not leave > 2 hours without consulting advanced provider
