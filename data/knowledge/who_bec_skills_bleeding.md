# WHO BEC Skills External Bleeding Control

## External Bleeding Control Assessment and Management

### Direct Pressure Management for External Bleeding
- Deep and heavily bleeding wound condition: Apply direct pressure as it may not stop on its own.
- PPE requirement: Put on gloves.
- Dressing selection: Use gauze or clean non-adherent dressing, and do not use bulky dressings.
- Pressure application: Apply firm pressure as directly as possible to the source of bleeding using two or three fingers.
- First dressing becomes blood-soaked: Do not remove the dressing to avoid dislodging clots. Add another pad on top and apply firm pressure.
- Bleeding stops: Apply a bandage to keep the gauze or pad in place.
- Bleeding does not stop with direct pressure: Consider deep wound packing or tourniquet application.
- Limb elevation consideration: Do not use limb elevation as a primary hemorrhage control measure. Maintain direct pressure as the priority and do not interrupt pressure to position the limb.

### Deep Wound Packing Management for External Bleeding
- Deep or gaping wound with uncontrolled bleeding after simple pressure: Apply deep wound packing.
- PPE requirement: Put on gloves.
- Perfusion assessment: Assess pulses, capillary refill, and sensation before and after dressing the wound.
- Wound cleaning: Flush the wound thoroughly with at least 1 litre of clean water, under pressure when possible.
- Packing material application: Use gauze or clean, compact material to completely fill the space within the wound.
- Pressure application after packing: Place additional gauze on top of the wound surface and apply direct pressure with a gloved hand or wrap a bandage firmly around the wound or limb.
- Limb wound requiring packing: Apply a splint to reduce the risk of re-bleeding.
- Deep wound pack duration limit: Do not leave the pack in place for more than 24 hours due to infection risk.
- Bleeding does not stop with deep wound packing: Consider tourniquet application.

### Tourniquet Application for Uncontrolled External Bleeding
- Tourniquet indication: Use ONLY if all other bleeding control measures have failed and haemorrhage is life-threatening. 
- Resource management: Call for help immediately and plan for handover and transfer to a surgical unit due to risk of permanent tissue damage or amputation.
- Pneumatic tourniquet available: Apply a pneumatic tourniquet (like a blood pressure cuff) over padded skin and inflate until bleeding stops.
- Pneumatic tourniquet unavailable: Use a thick band, cloth, or belt (wider is better) over padded skin.
- Tourniquet placement restriction: Apply as close to the wound as possible, but do not place over a wound or fracture. Apply on extremities ONLY and above the level of bleeding. Prefer the upper arm or leg for higher effectiveness compared to below the elbow or knee.
- Tourniquet pressure application: Apply enough pressure to make distal pulses disappear and reassess bleeding.
- Bleeding stops with tourniquet: Dress the wound and prepare for transfer to surgical care.
- Bleeding does not stop with initial tourniquet pressure: Increase tourniquet pressure until major bleeding ceases.
- Tourniquet documentation: Record the exact application time on the patient's skin or the tourniquet itself. Ensure the tourniquet is clearly visible.
- Medical consultation requirement: Consult an advanced provider as soon as possible, and never more than 2 hours after placing the tourniquet.
- Pre-hospital tourniquet management protocol (TCCC/MARCH): Do not release or loosen a tourniquet in the pre-hospital setting to avoid fatal re-bleeding. Transport to definitive surgical care with the tourniquet in place.

## Circulation Examination for External Bleeding
- Mental status assessment: Check for anxiety, confusion, or altered mental status.
- Pulse assessment: Feel for pulse rate and quality (normal adult rate is 60 to 100 beats per minute).
- Capillary refill assessment: Push on the fingernail, palm, or sole to assess refill time (normal return is less than 3 seconds).
- Skin assessment: Assess skin colour and temperature.
- Vital signs assessment: Measure respiratory rate and blood pressure (normal adult respiratory rate is 10 to 20 breaths per minute; normal systolic blood pressure is greater than 90 mmHg).
