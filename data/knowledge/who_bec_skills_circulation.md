## Circulation Skill: IV Cannulation

### Insertion of IV cannula (adult)
Indication: any adult with signs and symptoms of shock — insert two large-bore cannulae (14 or 16 gauge).
1. Prepare: cannula, IV fluid, tourniquet, gloves, dressing, alcohol swab.
2. Put on gloves; place elastic band/tourniquet on arm to engorge veins.
3. Avoid arms with fistulae (kidney disease patients).
4. Look and feel for a straight vein; avoid pulsating blood vessels.
5. If in shock and vein hard to find: search antecubital fossa (elbow bend).
6. Clean skin with alcohol. Do NOT insert through broken or infected skin.
7. Tell patient what you are doing.
8. Insert needle flat and parallel to skin, in line with vein. Watch for flashback (blood in cannula).
9. If blood has regular pumping pattern: you have hit an artery — remove and apply firm pressure 5–10 min.
10. After flashback: advance needle few mm further, then slide plastic cannula over needle fully into vein.
11. Hold cannula, apply pressure to base, withdraw needle; flush with normal saline; secure and dress site.
12. Dispose of needle into sharps container.
13. Check IV site daily for signs of infection: redness, pain, swelling.

### Insertion of IV cannula (paediatric)
Sites: hand (first choice), scalp veins, external jugular, antecubital, femoral, ankle veins.
- Prepare 21 or 23 gauge cannula.
- Keep hand bent to obstruct venous return and make veins visible.
- Same insertion technique as adults; withdraw blood if needed for lab; remove tourniquet; flush with saline.
- Secure well — children will attempt to remove. Avoid tape going completely around a limb.
- Splint joints near the IV to prevent dislodgement.
- Check IV site daily.

## Circulation Skill: IV Fluid Administration

### Fluid administration for shock in adults
1. Attach normal saline or Lactated Ringer's to cannula.
2. Adults: give **1 litre over less than 30 minutes**.
3. Assess response immediately after bolus: capillary refill, mental status, urine output, pulse rate, blood pressure.
4. Assess for fluid overload.
5. If still in shock with no evidence of fluid overload: give another 1 litre bolus.
6. If still in shock after 2 litres: reassess for ongoing blood loss (external and internal) or spinal injury; call for advanced provider.

### Fluid administration for shock in children (without severe malnutrition, anaemia, or overload)
1. Insert IV cannula; weigh the child.
2. Give normal saline or Lactated Ringer's: **10–20 ml/kg over 30 minutes**.
3. Reassess after first infusion. If no improvement: repeat 10 ml/kg over 30 minutes.
4. If shock persists: call for help; plan handover to advanced provider with blood transfusion capability.

### Fluid administration for child in shock WITH severe malnutrition, anaemia, or overload
Use specialized fluids if available:
- Ringer's Lactate with 5% glucose, OR Half-strength Darrow's with 5% glucose, OR 0.45% saline with 5% glucose.
- Give **10–15 ml/kg IV over 1 hour**. Reassess every 5–10 minutes.
- CAUTION: Check every 5 minutes for danger signs: new/worsening difficulty breathing, respiratory rate increase > 5/min or heart rate increase > 15 beats/min. Stop fluids if any danger sign develops.

## Circulation Skill: IV Fluid Adjustment for Special Conditions

Shock should be treated with IV fluids; however, adjust volume for:

### 1. Fluid Overload
Signs: difficulty breathing with crackles on chest exam; lower body swelling (legs); unable to lie flat; distended neck veins.
Adjustment: small boluses (250–500 ml in adults); slow rate; monitor closely; stop if signs worsen.
**CRITICAL:** Too much fluid can fill lungs and cause severe difficulty breathing or death.

### 2. Severe Anaemia
Signs: severe pallor to palms/mucous membranes; fast breathing or fast heart rate; confusion or restlessness; may have signs of heart failure.
Adjustment: slow rate; monitor closely; stop for worsening; rapid handover for blood transfusion.

### 3. Severe Malnutrition
Signs: visible wasting (bony, skin appears too large for body); bilateral foot oedema (press top of foot — dent remains).
Adjustment: prefer oral fluids if tolerated; add dextrose; slow rate; monitor closely; switch to oral as soon as possible.
**IMPORTANT:** For high-risk adults: bolus 500 ml IV fluid, reassess; if no fluid overload signs, give additional 500 ml.

## Circulation Skill: Uterine Massage for Postpartum Haemorrhage

After delivery, failure of the uterus to contract causes most abnormal postpartum bleeding. Call for help, arrange rapid handover/transfer, start uterine massage and give oxytocin immediately.

### Postpartum Haemorrhage Protocol
1. Arrange immediate transfer to qualified obstetric provider.
2. Massage uterus until hard. Give oxytocin IM. Give IV fluids and IV oxytocin. Empty bladder.
3. Continue uterine massage. If placenta not delivered: when uterus is hard, placenta will likely deliver. Collect placenta and keep with patient. Continue oxytocin.
4. If perineal/vaginal tear: apply pressure with sterile gauze; put legs together; continue oxytocin for at least 1 hour after bleeding stops.
5. If still bleeding: continue IV fluids with oxytocin; insert second IV line; transfer immediately.

### Performing Uterine Massage
- Goal: compress uterus between hand and bony structures behind it (sacrum/lower back).
- Place hand on woman's abdomen; cup uterus through abdominal wall; apply strong pressure toward patient's back while massaging with circular motion.
- Massage until uterus is very firm (feels like a 10 cm rock in lower abdomen).
- Do NOT stop massaging until uterus is contracted (hard).
- If uterus relaxes after stopping: resume massage.
- Continuously reassess for vaginal bleeding; perform frequent vital signs.

## Skill Assessment: Circulation

**Skill 1:** Circulation exam — look for AMS; pulse rate/quality; capillary refill; skin colour/temperature; vital signs

**Skill 5:** Uterine massage — call for help and initiate transfer; prepare oxytocin and IV fluid; compress uterus between hand and sacrum; massage until hard; do not stop until contracted; reassess continuously

**Skill 6:** Inserting IV cannula — gloves; tourniquet on limb; identify straight vein; clean skin; flat insertion; flashback; advance cannula over needle; remove needle; flush; secure; dispose sharps; check site daily

**Skill 7:** Recognizing conditions for IV fluid adjustment — fluid overload (crackles, lower body swelling, unable to lie flat, distended neck veins); severe anaemia; severe malnutrition (bilateral oedema assessment)

**Skill 8:** IV fluid resuscitation — adults: 1L normal saline or Ringer's over < 30 min; reassess; if no improvement repeat; if shock after 2L suspect ongoing blood loss; paediatric: 10–20 ml/kg over 30 min
