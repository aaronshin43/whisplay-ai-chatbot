# Stroke — Recognition and First Aid Response

Source: WHO Basic Emergency Care 2018, American Stroke Association, IFRC Guidelines

---

## Overview

A stroke occurs when blood supply to part of the brain is cut off. Brain cells begin to die within minutes without oxygen.

**Two types:**
- **Ischaemic stroke** (most common, ~85%): blood clot blocks an artery
- **Haemorrhagic stroke** (~15%): blood vessel ruptures and bleeds into the brain

**Critical principle:** "Time is brain." Every minute of delay causes the death of approximately 1.9 million brain cells. Rapid recognition and emergency response saves lives and reduces disability.

---

## Recognition — The FAST Assessment

### FAST Check

| Letter | Meaning | What to Look For |
|--------|---------|-----------------|
| **F** | Face | Is one side of the face drooping? Ask the person to smile — is it uneven? |
| **A** | Arms | Can they raise both arms? Does one arm drift downward? |
| **S** | Speech | Is speech slurred, strange, or difficult to understand? Can they repeat a simple sentence? |
| **T** | Time | Time to call emergency services IMMEDIATELY. Note the time symptoms started. |

**If ANY of these signs are present: CALL EMERGENCY SERVICES NOW (911/999/112)**

---

## Assessment — Additional Signs and Symptoms

### Signs of Stroke

- Sudden severe headache with no known cause ("worst headache of my life")
- Sudden numbness or weakness of face, arm, or leg — especially on ONE side of the body
- Sudden confusion or difficulty understanding
- Sudden vision problems in one or both eyes (blurred, double, or loss of vision)
- Sudden difficulty walking, dizziness, loss of balance or coordination
- Sudden facial drooping, especially on one side
- Sudden difficulty swallowing

### Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA — "Mini-Stroke")

- Same symptoms as stroke but resolve within minutes to hours
- TIA is a WARNING sign — **high risk of full stroke within 48 hours**
- Seek emergency evaluation IMMEDIATELY even if symptoms resolve

## First Aid Management for Stroke

### Immediate Actions

### Call Emergency Services First

1. **CALL emergency services (911/999/112) IMMEDIATELY** — do not wait to see if symptoms improve.
2. Note the exact time symptoms started — this is critical for treatment decisions (thrombolysis window is 3–4.5 hours from symptom onset).

### Position and Comfort

3. Help the person to lie down or sit in a comfortable position.
4. If conscious and breathing: position with head and shoulders slightly raised.
5. If unconscious but breathing: place in recovery position (on side).
6. Loosen tight clothing around the neck.
7. Keep the person warm with a blanket.

### Do NOT

- Do **NOT give aspirin** — stroke may be haemorrhagic (aspirin worsens bleeding strokes).
- Do **NOT give food or water** — swallowing may be impaired, risk of aspiration.
- Do **NOT leave the person alone**.
- Do **NOT allow the person to "sleep it off"** — symptoms will not improve without treatment.

---

## Monitoring Until Emergency Services Arrive

- Check breathing and pulse regularly.
- If breathing stops: begin CPR.
- Reassure the person — they may be frightened and confused.
- Note and report any changes in symptoms to emergency services.
- Keep bystanders calm.

---

## Information to Give Emergency Services

- Exact time symptoms started (or when person was last seen normal)
- Which FAST signs are present
- Any history of previous stroke, TIA, or blood thinners
- Current medications

---

## Haemorrhagic Stroke (Brain Bleed)

If the stroke is haemorrhagic (bleeding into brain):
- Aspirin and blood thinners are **contraindicated** (worsen outcome)
- Patient may have severe headache and vomiting
- Blood pressure is often very elevated
- Management is supportive until emergency care — no first aid intervention changes this

---

## Recovery Position for Unconscious Stroke Patient

1. Kneel beside the person.
2. Place the arm nearest to you at a right angle to their body.
3. Bring the far arm across their chest; hold back of their hand against their cheek.
4. With your other hand, pull the far knee up.
5. Roll the person towards you onto their side.
6. Tilt head back to keep airway open.
7. Monitor breathing continuously.
