# Diabetic Emergency — First Aid Response

Source: WHO Basic Emergency Care 2018 (Module 5), IFRC First Aid Guidelines

---

## Overview

People with diabetes can develop dangerous blood sugar emergencies in either direction:
- **Hypoglycaemia** (low blood sugar) — most common and most immediately dangerous
- **Hyperglycaemia / DKA** (high blood sugar / diabetic ketoacidosis) — develops over hours to days

---

## Hypoglycaemia (Low Blood Sugar)

### Definition
Blood glucose below 3.9 mmol/L (70 mg/dL). The brain depends entirely on glucose and deteriorates rapidly without it.

### Signs and Symptoms

**Mild to moderate:**
- Shakiness or trembling
- Sweating (diaphoresis) — often cold and clammy
- Rapid heartbeat (palpitations)
- Anxiety, nervousness, irritability
- Hunger
- Pale skin
- Headache
- Confusion or difficulty concentrating

**Severe:**
- Marked confusion or disorientation
- Slurred speech (may be mistaken for intoxication)
- Weakness
- Loss of coordination
- Seizures
- Unconsciousness

### First Aid Management — Hypoglycaemia

#### Conscious Person — Can Swallow Safely

1. Give **15–20 grams of fast-acting carbohydrate** immediately:
   - 4 glucose tablets
   - Half a cup (125 mL) of fruit juice or regular (not diet) soda
   - 1 tablespoon of honey or sugar
   - Glucose gel (if available)

2. **Wait 15 minutes**, then recheck symptoms.
3. If symptoms do not improve after 15 minutes: **repeat the sugar dose**.
4. Once symptoms improve: give a small meal or snack containing protein and complex carbohydrates.
5. If in doubt about consciousness or ability to swallow: **do NOT give anything by mouth**.

#### Unconscious or Unable to Swallow

1. **Do NOT give anything by mouth** — risk of aspiration (choking).
2. Place the person in recovery position (on their side).
3. **Call emergency services (911/999/112) immediately**.
4. If glucagon is available and you are trained to use it: administer IM glucagon.
5. Monitor breathing until emergency services arrive.

## Hyperglycaemia (High Blood Sugar)

### Signs and Symptoms

- Excessive thirst (polydipsia)
- Frequent urination (polyuria)
- Blurred vision
- Fatigue and weakness
- Headache
- Fruity or acetone odour on breath (DKA)
- Nausea and vomiting
- Abdominal pain
- Deep, laboured breathing (Kussmaul breathing in DKA)
- Gradual onset over hours to days

### First Aid Management — Hyperglycaemia

1. If the person is conscious and can manage their own insulin: encourage them to check blood glucose and administer their prescribed insulin.
2. Encourage the person to drink water (not sugary drinks).
3. **Do NOT give insulin yourself** unless specifically trained and authorised.
4. If the person is very ill, vomiting, or has altered consciousness: **call emergency services immediately**.
5. Hyperglycaemia is a slower emergency than hypoglycaemia but still requires medical evaluation.

---

## Differentiation: Hypoglycaemia vs Hyperglycaemia

| Feature | Hypoglycaemia (Low Sugar) | Hyperglycaemia (High Sugar) |
|---------|--------------------------|------------------------------|
| Onset | Rapid (minutes) | Gradual (hours to days) |
| Skin | Pale, cold, sweaty | Warm, dry, flushed |
| Breath | Normal | Fruity/acetone odour |
| Breathing | Normal | Deep, rapid (DKA) |
| Confusion | Sudden | Gradual |
| Treatment | Give sugar | Seek medical care; avoid giving sugar |

**If uncertain:** Give sugar first. If the person has hyperglycaemia, giving a small amount of sugar will not cause significant harm. If they have hypoglycaemia, not giving sugar can be life-threatening.

---

## When to Call Emergency Services

Call 911/999/112 immediately if the person:
- Is unconscious or unresponsive
- Is having a seizure
- Does not improve after two doses of sugar (15 minutes each)
- Has signs of DKA (fruity breath, deep breathing, vomiting)
- Has any difficulty breathing
- Is a child under 5 years with low blood sugar

---

## Diabetic Person with Altered Mental Status

If a diabetic person is confused or behaving strangely and you are unsure of the cause:
1. **Assume hypoglycaemia first** if the person can swallow.
2. Give sugar (juice, glucose tablets).
3. Call emergency services if no improvement within 15 minutes.
4. Do NOT withhold sugar out of concern for high blood sugar — hypoglycaemia kills faster.
