# Seizure and Epilepsy — First Aid Response

Source: WHO Basic Emergency Care 2018 (Module 5), IFRC First Aid Guidelines

---

## Overview of Seizures

A seizure is a sudden, uncontrolled burst of electrical activity in the brain. It may cause changes in behaviour, movement, feeling, or consciousness.

**Common causes include:**
- Epilepsy (recurrent seizures)
- Febrile seizure (high fever in children)
- Head injury or brain trauma
- Low blood sugar (hypoglycaemia)
- Stroke or cerebrovascular event
- Drug or alcohol toxicity
- Electrolyte imbalance

---

## Assessment — Recognising a Seizure

### Signs and Symptoms of Generalised Tonic-Clonic Seizure

- Sudden loss of consciousness
- Stiffening of the body (tonic phase)
- Rhythmic jerking of the arms and legs (clonic phase)
- Clenched teeth or jaw
- Loss of bladder or bowel control
- Brief period of confusion or disorientation after the seizure (post-ictal state)
- Duration usually 1–3 minutes

### Signs of Focal (Partial) Seizure

- Jerking of one limb or one side of the face
- Blank staring or unresponsive episode
- Lip-smacking, chewing, or repetitive movements
- Person may appear confused but not collapse

---

## First Aid Management for Seizures

### Immediate Actions — During the Seizure

### Ensure Safety

1. **Do NOT restrain or hold the person down.** Restraining can cause injuries.
2. Clear the area of hard, sharp, or dangerous objects that could cause injury.
3. Cushion the head with something soft (folded jacket, bag).
4. Remove glasses if worn.
5. Loosen any tight clothing around the neck.

### Do NOT Attempt

- Do NOT put anything in the person's mouth — NOT fingers, NOT a spoon, NOT any object.
- Do NOT give water or food during the seizure.
- Do NOT try to stop the jerking movements.
- Do NOT leave the person unattended if they are alone.

---

### After the Seizure Stops

### Recovery Position

1. Once jerking stops, gently roll the person onto their side (recovery position).
2. This prevents choking if vomit occurs.
3. Keep the airway clear — tilt head back slightly, chin forward.
4. Check for breathing. If not breathing, begin CPR.

### Monitor

- Time how long the seizure lasted.
- Monitor the person's breathing and level of consciousness.
- Stay calm and reassure the person as they regain awareness.
- The person may be confused, tired, or disoriented for 10–30 minutes (post-ictal).

---

## When to Call Emergency Services

### Call 911/999/112 Immediately If:

- Seizure lasts more than **5 minutes**
- Another seizure occurs within **30 minutes**
- The person does NOT regain consciousness after the seizure
- The person is injured during the seizure
- The person has difficulty breathing after the seizure
- The seizure occurs in water (drowning risk)
- The person is **pregnant**
- The person has **diabetes**
- This is the person's **first known seizure**

## Febrile Seizure (Children)

Febrile seizures occur in children aged 6 months to 5 years with high fever.

### Assessment
- Usually brief (less than 5 minutes)
- Child stiffens, then jerks rhythmically
- Eyes may roll back
- Child may lose consciousness briefly

### Management

1. Place child on a safe surface; clear the area of hazards.
2. Do NOT restrain the child.
3. Do NOT put anything in the mouth.
4. Time the seizure.
5. After the seizure: place child in recovery position (on side).
6. Cool the child if fever is high — remove excess clothing, apply cool (not cold) cloths.
7. **Seek medical evaluation** after any febrile seizure.

---

## Status Epilepticus

**Definition:** Seizure lasting more than 5 minutes, OR two or more seizures without regaining consciousness between them.

Status epilepticus is a **medical emergency**.

### Action

1. Call emergency services IMMEDIATELY.
2. Protect the person from injury.
3. Maintain airway — recovery position.
4. Monitor breathing continuously.
5. Do NOT leave the person.

---

## Post-Seizure Care

- Allow the person to rest; they may be exhausted.
- Explain what happened calmly — they may have no memory of the seizure.
- Advise the person NOT to drive until cleared by a physician.
- Encourage follow-up with a healthcare provider if this was a new or unusual seizure.
