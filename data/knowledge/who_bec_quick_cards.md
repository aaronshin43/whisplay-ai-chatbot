# WHO BEC Quick Reference — Critical Actions, Drug Dosages, and Transfer

---

## ABCDE Quick Reference Card

### A — Airway

- Unconscious with limited or no air movement, no trauma: Head-tilt chin-lift; place OPA or NPA; recovery position or position of comfort
- Unconscious with limited or no air movement, possible trauma: Jaw thrust with C-spine protection; place OPA (no NPA if facial trauma)
- Foreign body in airway: Remove if visible; encourage coughing; if unable to cough — age-appropriate chest/abdominal thrusts or back blows; patient becomes unconscious — start CPR
- Gurgling: Open airway as above; suction (avoid triggering gag reflex)
- Stridor: Keep patient calm; allow position of comfort; signs of anaphylaxis → give IM adrenaline; hypoxia present → give oxygen

### B — Breathing

- Abnormal breathing or hypoxia: Give oxygen; assist ventilation with BVM if breathing is not adequate
- Wheeze: Give salbutamol; signs of anaphylaxis → give IM adrenaline
- Tension pneumothorax (absent sounds or hyperresonance on one side WITH hypotension and distended neck veins): Needle decompression immediately; give oxygen and IV fluids; chest tube required
- Opioid overdose signs (AMS + slow breathing + small pupils): Give naloxone

### C — Circulation

- No pulse: Follow relevant CPR protocol
- Signs of poor perfusion or shock: Give oxygen and IV fluids
- Internal or external bleeding: Control external bleeding; give IV fluids
- Pericardial tamponade (poor perfusion + distended neck veins + muffled heart sounds): Give IV fluids and oxygen; rapid pericardial drainage required

### D — Disability

- Altered mental status, no trauma: Place in recovery position
- Seizure: Give benzodiazepine
- Seizure in pregnancy or after recent delivery: Give magnesium sulphate
- Hypoglycaemia (blood glucose < 3.5 mmol/L or unknown): Give glucose
- Opioid overdose signs (AMS + slow breathing + small pupils): Give naloxone
- Life-threatening brain mass or bleed (AMS + unequal pupils): Raise head of bed; monitor airway; rapid transfer for neurosurgical care

### E — Exposure

- Remove wet clothing; dry skin thoroughly
- Remove jewellery, watches, and all constricting clothing
- Prevent hypothermia; protect modesty
- Snake bite: Immobilise the extremity; send photograph of the snake with the patient; call for antivenom if available
- Unknown cause: Examine entire body; always consider hidden injuries

**All patients with abnormal ABCDE findings may need rapid transfer — plan early.**

## Trauma Quick Reference Card

### Trauma Primary Survey — Key ABCDE Findings and Actions

**A — Airway (with C-spine protection):**
- Not speaking with limited or no air movement: Jaw thrust with C-spine protection; suction; remove visible foreign objects; place OPA
- Possible airway injury (neck haematoma or wound, crepitus, stridor): Give oxygen; monitor closely — swelling can rapidly block airway; advanced airway management required
- Possible airway burns (soot around mouth or nose, burned facial hair, facial burns): Give oxygen; monitor closely — airway can close rapidly; advanced airway management required

**B — Breathing:**
- Tension pneumothorax (hypotension + absent breath sounds or hyperresonance on one side + distended neck veins): Needle decompression immediately; give oxygen and IV fluids; chest tube required
- Sucking chest wound (open pneumothorax): Give oxygen; apply three-sided dressing; monitor for tension pneumothorax; chest tube required
- Inadequate breathing: Give oxygen; assist with BVM ventilation
- Large or circumferential burns to chest or abdomen: Give IV fluids per burn size; give oxygen; remove constricting clothing and jewellery; may need escharotomy
- Flail chest (chest wall section moving in opposite direction during breathing): Give oxygen; may need advanced airway and assisted ventilation
- Haemothorax (decreased breath sounds on one side, dull percussion): Give oxygen and IV fluids; chest tube required

**C — Circulation:**
- Shock (capillary refill > 3 seconds, hypotension, tachycardia): Give oxygen and IV fluids; control external bleeding; splint femur or pelvis as indicated
- Uncontrolled external bleeding: Direct pressure; deep wound packing; or tourniquet as indicated
- Pericardial tamponade (poor perfusion + distended neck veins + muffled heart sounds): Give IV fluids and oxygen; rapid transfer required

**D — Disability:**
- Brain injury signs (AMS + head wound, deformity, or bruising): Immobilise cervical spine; check glucose; give nothing by mouth; neurosurgical care required
- Open skull fracture (as above + blood or fluid from ears or nose): As above, plus give IV antibiotics per local protocol

### Trauma — Specific Injury Management

- **Facial fracture:** Immobilise cervical spine if indicated; give IV antibiotics for open fractures; avoid nasal airways and nasogastric tubes
- **Penetrating eye injury:** Apply no pressure to the eye; stabilise but do not remove foreign objects; give antibiotics and tetanus vaccination; elevate head of bed
- **Open abdominal wound:** Give IV fluids; give nothing by mouth; cover visible bowel with sterile saline-soaked gauze; give antibiotics
- **Pelvic fracture:** Give IV fluids; stabilise with sheet or pelvic binder
- **Fracture with poor limb perfusion:** Reduce fracture; splint
- **Open fracture:** Irrigate well; dress wound; splint; give antibiotics; arrange urgent operative management
- **Penetrating object:** Leave in place; stabilise to prevent further movement
- **Crush injury:** Give IV fluids; monitor urine output; monitor for compartment syndrome
- **Burn injury:** Assess burn size; calculate fluid needs; give IV fluids and oxygen; monitor for airway oedema
- **Blast injury:** Give oxygen; treat burns; give IV fluids; monitor closely for delayed effects of internal injury

**All wounds including burns and open fractures require tetanus vaccination.**

### High-Risk Mechanisms and Injuries

**High-risk mechanisms:**
- Pedestrian or cyclist struck by a vehicle
- Motorcycle crash or vehicle crash with unrestrained occupants
- Fall from > 3 metres (or twice a child's height)
- Gunshot or stab wound
- Explosion or fire in an enclosed space

**High-risk injuries:**
- Penetrating injuries to head, neck, or torso
- Blast or crush injuries
- Flail chest
- Two or more large bone fractures, or pelvic fracture
- Spinal injury with limb paralysis
- Amputation above wrist or ankle

### Trauma Disposition — Conditions Requiring Transfer to Specialist Unit

- Any ABCDE finding requiring intervention
- Altered mental status
- Evidence of internal bleeding
- Any pneumothorax or sucking chest wound
- Shock — even if successfully treated
- Trauma in pregnancy
- ABCDE abnormalities or any chest or abdominal injury in a child
- Significant burn injuries

**During transfer:** Continue oxygen if it has been required; maintain IV fluids if shock is present; control and monitor all external bleeding sites.

## Difficulty in Breathing Quick Reference Card

### Key ABCDE Findings in DIB

| Finding | Think of |
|---|---|
| Choking or coughing | Foreign body |
| Stridor | Partial upper airway obstruction — foreign body, infection, chemical, or burn |
| Facial swelling | Severe allergic reaction or medication effect |
| Drooling | Blocked swallowing — severe airway obstruction |
| Soot around mouth or nose, burned facial hair, facial burns | Smoke inhalation and airway burns — rapid swelling risk |
| Chest wall trauma signs | Rib fracture, flail chest, pneumothorax, cardiac contusion, tamponade |
| Decreased breath sounds on one side | Pneumothorax (tension if with hypotension and hyperresonance), haemothorax, large pleural effusion, or pneumonia |
| Decreased breath sounds and crackles on both sides | Pulmonary oedema or heart failure |
| Wheezing | Asthma, allergic reaction, or COPD |
| Fast or deep breathing | DKA |
| Hypotension + tachycardia + muffled heart sounds | Pericardial tamponade |
| AMS + small pupils + slow breathing | Opioid overdose |

### Key SAMPLE History Findings in DIB

| Finding | Think of |
|---|---|
| DIB worse with exertion | Heart failure or heart attack |
| DIB started during eating or choking | Foreign body or allergic reaction |
| Fever and cough | Pneumonia or infection |
| Pesticide exposure | Poisoning |
| Recent fall or trauma | Rib fracture, flail chest, pneumothorax, contusion, tamponade |
| Known allergies, allergen exposure, bite, or sting | Allergic reaction |
| Recent medication or dose change | Allergic reaction or side effect |
| History of opioid or sedative drug use | Overdose |
| History of wheezing | Asthma or COPD |
| History of diabetes | DKA |
| History of TB or malignancy | Pericardial tamponade or pleural effusion |
| History of heart failure | Pulmonary oedema |
| History of sickle cell disease | Acute chest syndrome |

### Critical Actions for DIB — By Condition

**Choking (unable to cough, not making sounds):**
1. Remove any visible foreign body
2. Age-appropriate chest/abdominal thrusts or back blows
3. Patient becomes unconscious: Start CPR

**Stridor:**
1. Keep patient calm; allow position of comfort
2. Suspected allergic reaction: Give IM adrenaline
3. Give oxygen if hypoxia present
4. Arrange early transfer for advanced airway management

**Wheezing:**
1. Give salbutamol
2. Suspected allergic reaction: Give IM adrenaline
3. Give oxygen if hypoxia present

**Severe Infection with DIB:**
1. Give oxygen
2. Give antibiotics
3. Give IV or oral fluids as appropriate

**Trauma with DIB:**
1. Give oxygen
2. Needle decompression for tension pneumothorax
3. Three-sided dressing for sucking chest wound
4. Arrange rapid transfer to surgical service

### Paediatric Danger Signs in DIB

- Fast breathing
- Increased breathing effort (chest indrawing or retractions)
- Cyanosis
- Altered mental status or lethargy
- Poor feeding or drinking; vomiting everything
- Recent or active seizures
- Drooling or stridor at rest
- Hypothermia

**Fast breathing may be the only sign of a serious breathing problem in a child.**

Stridor in children = object stuck in the airway or airway swelling from infection.
Wheeze in children = inhaled object, viral infection, or asthma.
Fast or deep breathing can indicate DKA — may be the first sign of diabetes in a child.

### DIB Disposition

- Salbutamol and IM adrenaline last approximately 3 hours — life-threatening symptoms can recur; monitor closely; have repeat doses available during transport; alert receiving providers
- Naloxone lasts approximately 1 hour — most opioids last longer; have repeat doses available; alert receiving providers
- Post-drowning: Delayed breathing problems can develop several hours after the event — monitor closely and alert receiving providers
- Never leave a DIB patient unmonitored during transfer
- Arrange transfer as early as possible for any patient who may need intubation or assisted ventilation

## Shock Quick Reference Card

### Key ABCDE Findings in Shock

| Finding | Think of |
|---|---|
| DIB + stridor or wheeze + skin rash + mouth swelling | Severe allergic reaction |
| Hypotension + absent breath sounds + hyperresonance on one side + distended neck veins | Tension pneumothorax |
| Distended neck veins + muffled heart sounds + tachycardia + hypotension | Pericardial tamponade |
| Sweet-smelling breath + deep or rapid breathing | DKA |
| History of trauma or no known cause | Hidden blood loss (GI tract, abdomen, chest, long bones) or spinal injury |

### Key SAMPLE History Findings in Shock

| Finding | Think of |
|---|---|
| Vomiting and diarrhoea | Ask about contacts; report per protocol |
| Black or bloody vomit or stool | Gastrointestinal bleeding |
| Rapid/deep breathing + dehydration + high glucose + sweet breath + polyuria | DKA |
| Burns | Severe fluid loss — calculate needs from burn size |
| Fever or HIV | Infection |
| Recent fall or trauma | Internal and external bleeding |
| Pale conjunctiva or malnutrition | Severe anaemia — adjust fluids |
| Chest pain | Heart attack — give aspirin if indicated |
| Vaginal bleeding | Pregnancy-related or other bleeding |
| Numbness, weakness, or shock not improving with fluids | Spinal shock — immobilise spine if indicated |

### Critical Actions for Shock — All Types

For all shock:
- Give oxygen
- Give IV fluids:
  - **Adults:** 1 litre Ringer's Lactate or Normal Saline bolus
  - **Children — no severe anaemia, no malnutrition, no fluid overload:** 10–20 mL/kg bolus
  - **Children — malnutrition or severe anaemia:** 10–15 mL/kg dextrose-containing fluid over 1 hour; assess for fluid overload every 5 minutes
  - **Suspected heart attack with shock:** Give smaller boluses; monitor closely for fluid overload
- Monitor vital signs, mental status, breathing, and urine output

### Critical Actions for Shock — Specific Conditions

| Condition | Action |
|---|---|
| Severe allergic reaction | IM adrenaline; monitor for recurrence; may need repeat doses |
| Tension pneumothorax | Needle decompression; transfer for chest tube |
| Pericardial tamponade | IV fluids; rapid transfer for drainage |
| Fever/infection | Antibiotics (and antimalarials if indicated); give IV fluids; check for cholera and notify public health if suspected |
| Postpartum haemorrhage | Oxytocin IM and IV; uterine massage; direct pressure for perineal or vaginal tears; rapid transfer to obstetric care |
| DKA | IV fluids; close monitoring; transfer for insulin |
| Trauma/haemorrhage | Control external bleeding; give IV fluids; calculate fluid needs from burn size if burns; rapid transfer for surgery or transfusion |

### Paediatric Shock Assessment

WHO 2016 definition of shock in children — all three features:
- Cold extremities
- Weak and fast pulse
- Capillary refill > 3 seconds

Additional poor perfusion signs: Sunken eyes, sunken fontanelles in infants, abnormal skin pinch, pallor, decreased dark urine, low BP, fast breathing, AMS, dry mouth and lips, lethargy

**Paediatric fluid dosing by weight:**

| Weight (kg) | Volume at 15 mL/kg |
|---|---|
| 4 | 60 mL |
| 6 | 90 mL |
| 10 | 150 mL |
| 14 | 210 mL |
| 20 | 300 mL |
| 30 | 450 mL |

Child in shock WITH severe malnutrition or fluid overload: Give 10–15 mL/kg dextrose-containing fluid over 1 hour.

### Shock Disposition

Transfer to a unit capable of IV fluid resuscitation, blood transfusion, and/or surgery depending on shock type. Maintain IV fluids during transport. Repeat ABCDE every 15 minutes; monitor perfusion and breathing continuously.

## Altered Mental Status Quick Reference Card

**Always check blood glucose in any patient with AMS — or give glucose if unable to check.**

### Key ABCDE Findings in AMS

| Finding | Think of |
|---|---|
| Tachypnoea | Hypoxia, DKA, or toxic ingestion |
| Tachycardia with normal perfusion | Alcohol withdrawal |
| Coma | Hypoxia, hypo/hyperglycaemia, DKA, or toxic ingestion |
| Hypoglycaemia | Infection, medication side effect (e.g., diabetes medications, quinine) |
| Very small pupils + slow breathing | Opioid overdose |
| Seizure or convulsion | Abnormal glucose, infection, toxic ingestion (e.g., TB medications), or withdrawal (e.g., alcohol); consider eclampsia if pregnant or recently delivered |
| One-sided weakness or unequal pupils | Brain mass or bleed |
| Signs of trauma or unknown AMS cause | Brain injury with possible spine injury |

### Key SAMPLE History Findings in AMS

| Finding | Think of |
|---|---|
| History of wheezing | Severe COPD crisis can cause AMS |
| History of diabetes | High or low blood glucose; DKA |
| History of epilepsy | Post-seizure confusion should improve over minutes to hours; prolonged AMS or multiple seizures without waking requires further workup |
| History of agricultural work or pesticide exposure | Organophosphate poisoning |
| History of regular alcohol use | Alcohol withdrawal |
| History of substance use or depression | Acute intoxication or accidental or intentional overdose |
| History of HIV | Infection or medication side effect |
| Rash on lower abdomen or legs, or bulging fontanelle in infant | Brain infection (meningitis) |
| Fever or hyperthermia | Infectious, toxic, or environmental cause |

### Critical Actions for AMS — By Condition

**Hypoglycaemia:**
1. Give glucose
2. Evaluate for underlying infection or cause
3. Monitor for return of hypoglycaemia

**Opioid Overdose:**
1. Give naloxone
2. Monitor for re-narcotisation — most opioids last longer than naloxone; have repeat doses available

**Life-Threatening Infection (fever or rash with AMS):**
1. Give IV fluids
2. Start antibiotics immediately
3. AMS + fever + rash: Suspect meningitis — isolate patient and wear a mask

**Severe Dehydration:**
1. Give IV fluids
2. Assess for DKA

**Toxic Exposure or Withdrawal:**
1. Gather full history
2. Consult advanced provider for locally appropriate antidotes
3. Alcohol withdrawal: Give benzodiazepine
4. Chemical exposure (e.g., pesticides): Decontaminate
5. Very high fever: Cool the patient — avoid shivering

### Paediatric Considerations for AMS

- Always consider unwitnessed toxic ingestion — ask about all medications and chemicals in or near the home
- Check blood glucose regularly — low glucose is common in ill young children; high glucose can present with AMS and dehydration
- Avoid hypothermia: Keep skin-to-skin with mother; cover the child's head; uncover only the parts being examined
- Ingestion danger signs requiring close monitoring and transfer: Stridor; oral chemical burns
- Monitor fluid status closely — paediatric patients are highly susceptible to both fluid loss and fluid overload

### AMS Disposition

- Any AMS patient who may not protect the airway: Never leave alone; monitor continuously; give direct handover to receiving provider
- Naloxone lasts approximately 1 hour — alert receiving providers that repeat doses may be needed
- Treated hypoglycaemia: Alert receiving providers to monitor blood glucose frequently — hypoglycaemia commonly recurs

## Drug Dosage Reference

### Adrenaline (Epinephrine) — 1 mg/mL ampoule (1:1000)

**Indication:** Anaphylaxis, severe allergic reaction, severe wheezing

**Adults (IM dose):**
- ≥ 50 kg: 0.5 mg IM (0.5 mL of 1:1000)
- 40 kg: 0.4 mg IM (0.4 mL)
- 30 kg: 0.3 mg IM (0.3 mL)
- Repeat every 5 minutes as needed

**Children (IM dose):**
- Anaphylaxis: 0.15 mg IM (0.15 mL of 1:1000); repeat every 5–15 minutes as needed
- Severe asthma: 0.01 mg/kg IM up to 0.3 mg maximum; repeat every 15 minutes as needed

---

### Aspirin (Acetylsalicylic Acid) — 100 mg or 300 mg tablets

**Indication:** Suspected heart attack

**Dose:** 300 mg as a single dose immediately — preferably chewed or dissolved in water

---

### Diazepam — 5 mg/mL ampoule; 2 mg and 5 mg tablets

**Indication:** Seizures and convulsions

**Adults:**
- First dose: 10 mg slow IV push, or 20 mg rectally
- Second dose after 10 minutes (if seizing): 5 mg slow IV push, or 10 mg rectally
- Maximum IV dose: 30 mg

**Children:**
- First dose: 0.2 mg/kg slow IV push, or 0.5 mg/kg rectally
- Repeat half the first dose after 10 minutes if seizures continue
- Maximum IV dose: 20 mg

**Monitor breathing closely in all patients given diazepam.**

---

### Glucose (Dextrose) — D50, D25, or D10 solutions

**Indication:** Hypoglycaemia (low blood glucose)

**Adults and children > 40 kg:**
- 25–50 mL IV of D50, or 125–250 mL IV of D10

**Children ≤ 40 kg:**
- D10 (preferred): 5 mL/kg IV
- D25: 2 mL/kg IV
- D50: 1 mL/kg IV

**No IV access:** 2–5 mL of 50% dextrose or sugar solution in the buccal space (inside the cheek)

---

### Magnesium Sulphate — 1 g/2 mL (50%) or 5 g/10 mL (50%) ampoule

**Indication:** Eclampsia or pregnant patient with seizure

**Loading dose:**
- Give 4 g IV: Dilute to a 20% solution; give 20 mL slowly over 20 minutes
- AND give 10 g IM: 5 g (10 mL of 50% solution) + 1 mL of 2% lidocaine in each buttock (5 g per side)
- Unable to give IV: Give 10 g IM only as above

**Recurrent seizures:** After 15 minutes give additional 2 g (10 mL of 20%) IV over 20 minutes

**Transport delayed — maintenance dose:** Give 5 g of 50% solution IM + 1 mL of 2% lidocaine every 4 hours in alternating buttocks

---

### Naloxone — 400 mcg/mL (1 mL ampoule)

**Indication:** Opioid overdose

**Dose:**
- IV: 100 mcg single dose
- IM: 400 mcg single dose
- Repeat every 5 minutes as needed
- Long-acting opioids may require 0.4 mg/hr infusion for several hours

---

### Oxytocin — 10 IU/mL ampoule

**Indication:** Postpartum haemorrhage

**Initial dose:** Give 10 IU IM AND start IV fluids with 20 IU/L at 60 drops/minute

**After placenta delivery — still bleeding:** Continue IV fluids with 20 IU/L at 30 drops/minute

**Placenta requires manual removal or uterus not contracting:** Repeat 10 IU IM; continue IV fluids with 20 IU/L at 20 drops/minute for 1 hour after bleeding stops

**Maximum dose:** 3 litres of IV fluids containing oxytocin

---

### Paracetamol (Acetaminophen) — 250 mg and 500 mg tablets or suppositories

**Indication:** Mild to moderate pain, fever, headache

**Adults:** 500 mg–1 g orally or rectally every 6 hours; maximum 4 g daily (maximum 2 g daily with liver impairment or cirrhosis)

**Children:** 10–15 mg/kg orally or rectally up to six times per day

---

### Salbutamol (Albuterol) — 100 mcg/puff inhaler; nebuliser solution

**Indication:** Severe wheezing

**Inhaler — Adults:** Prime with 5 puffs; give 2 puffs via spacer every 2 minutes until improved

**Inhaler — Children:** Prime with 5 puffs; give 2 puffs into spacer; keep spacer in mouth for 3–5 breaths; repeat until 6 puffs given (< 5 years) or 12 puffs (> 5 years)

**Nebuliser — Adults:** 5 mg in 5 mL sterile saline

**Nebuliser — Children:** 2.5 mg in 3 mL sterile saline

For severe wheezing: Above doses can be repeated several times within one hour.

---

### Tetanus Vaccine — 0.5 mL IM

**Indication:** Any wound including burns and open fractures

**Give to:** Children not up to date on vaccination; adults with no tetanus vaccination in the past 5 years; or vaccination status unknown

## Transfer and Handover Reference

### Arranging Transfer

- Confirm the destination facility can provide the services the patient needs (operating theatre, blood products, specialist care)
- Communicate directly with an accepting provider at the receiving facility before departure
- Confirm the patient's condition allows for the time required to reach the destination
- Ensure patient and family know the reason, plan, and destination for transport
- Record family contact name and number in both the sending chart and transfer paperwork
- Secure patient valuables — whenever possible, leave with family
- A brief written record (name, date of birth, clinical presentation, and all interventions) must always accompany the patient

### Preparing for Transport

- PPE for all transport staff
- Airway equipment and suction — confirm working before departure
- Adequate oxygen supply with a replacement tank if needed; BVM
- IV access confirmed and secured before departure; consider second IV or backup supply
- Bring additional doses of all medications and fluids; anticipate medications that may be needed en route
- Anticipate new or recurrent symptoms during transport:
  - Seizure patients: Place padding around the patient to limit injury
  - Vomiting risk: Monitor airway continuously — especially with cervical spine immobilisation
- Confirm adequate fuel for transport
- Confirm telephone or radio is present and working in the vehicle

### Patient Positioning During Transport

- Position for best airway opening and breathing
- No trauma: Use recovery position
- Pregnancy > 20 weeks, no spinal injury: Place pillows along the right back to tilt patient onto left side — prevents vena cava compression by the gravid uterus
- Confirmed or suspected spinal injury: Use backboard with log-roll technique; check for pressure spots every 2 hours and pad as needed; pregnancy > 20 weeks on backboard: Tip board slightly to the left using a wedge
- Fractures: Splint and immobilise to protect soft tissue and reduce pain and bleeding

### Ongoing Care During Transport

- Reassess ABCDE at least every 15 minutes — include repeat vital signs and glucose checks if the patient has been hypoglycaemic
- Control all bleeding before departure; monitor all bleeding sites during transport
- Reassess all splinted extremities regularly
- Continue all necessary treatments (oxygen, IV fluids, oxytocin, glucose)
- Maintain patient temperature — prevent both overheating and hypothermia

### Paediatric Transport Considerations

- Prepare age-appropriate equipment and weight-adjusted medication dosages before departure
- Bring a family member or friend; inform the receiving facility who is accompanying the child
- Critically ill or injured children can appear well initially and then deteriorate rapidly — monitor continuously
- Hypothermia and hypoglycaemia are common in children — monitor closely throughout transport

### SBAR Handover Format

- **S — Situation:** Patient age and sex; chief complaint (e.g., difficulty breathing for 3 days, or arm pain after a fall)
- **B — Background:** 2–4 most important and relevant aspects of the case and condition; key ABCDE findings and interventions performed
- **A — Assessment:** What you believe is wrong with the patient; reason for the transfer
- **R — Recommendations:** Next treatment steps; anticipated complications (e.g., close airway monitoring if inhalation burn suspected); cautions regarding prior therapies — time of last adrenaline dose, need to monitor mental status after sedating medications, need to monitor three-sided dressing for clotting, need for repeat naloxone doses
