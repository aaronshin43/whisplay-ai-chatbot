# Chest Pain and Cardiac Emergencies — WHO BEC Reference

---

## Acute Coronary Syndrome (Heart Attack / ACS)

### Signs and Symptoms of ACS

**Classic presentation:**
- Chest pain or pressure — crushing, squeezing, or tight sensation
- Pain radiating to left arm, jaw, neck, or back
- Shortness of breath
- Diaphoresis (profuse sweating)
- Nausea or vomiting
- Anxiety or feeling of impending doom
- Pale, cool, clammy skin

**Atypical presentation** (common in women, elderly, and diabetic patients):
- Epigastric pain or indigestion
- Fatigue or weakness without chest pain
- Silent MI — no chest pain at all

### OPQRST Chest Pain Assessment

- **O — Onset:** Sudden or gradual?
- **P — Provocation/Palliation:** Worse with exertion? Relieved by rest or nitrates?
- **Q — Quality:** Crushing, stabbing, burning, or pressure?
- **R — Radiation:** Does it spread to the arm, jaw, or back?
- **S — Severity:** Rate 0–10
- **T — Time:** How long has it been present?

### Care and Management for ACS

1. Position: Sit the patient upright or in position of comfort; eliminate all exertion
2. Oxygen: Give supplemental O₂ if SpO₂ < 94% or signs of respiratory distress are present
3. Aspirin: Give **300–325 mg aspirin** crushed or chewed (not swallowed whole) if:
   - No known aspirin allergy
   - No active GI bleeding
   - Patient can swallow
4. Nitroglycerine: If prescribed and available — 0.4 mg sublingual; may repeat every 5 minutes up to 3 doses
   - **Do not give** if systolic BP < 90 mmHg or patient took a phosphodiesterase inhibitor (e.g., sildenafil) within the past 24 hours
5. Establish IV access if trained and equipment is available
6. Monitor pulse oximetry and repeat vitals every 5 minutes
7. Activate EMS immediately — do not delay transport for any intervention
8. Do not allow the patient to walk — carry or use a stretcher

## Acute Heart Failure (Pulmonary Oedema)

### Signs and Symptoms of Acute Heart Failure

- Severe difficulty breathing, especially when lying flat (orthopnoea)
- Patient prefers sitting upright or leaning forward
- Pink, frothy sputum (severe cases)
- Crackles (crepitations) heard at lung bases
- Distended neck veins (JVD)
- Peripheral oedema (swollen ankles or legs)
- Pale or cyanotic skin
- Anxiety and agitation
- History of hypertension or prior heart disease

### Care and Management for Acute Heart Failure

1. Position: Sit upright with legs dangling — reduces venous return and cardiac preload
2. Oxygen: High-flow O₂ via face mask; target SpO₂ ≥ 94%
3. **Do not lay the patient flat** — this severely worsens breathing
4. Monitor airway, breathing, and consciousness continuously
5. Activate EMS immediately — hospital care (diuretics, CPAP) is required

## Pericardial Tamponade

### Signs and Symptoms of Pericardial Tamponade

**Beck's Triad (classic):**
- Hypotension (low blood pressure)
- Distended neck veins (JVD)
- Muffled heart sounds

**Additional signs:**
- Rapid, weak pulse
- Narrowed pulse pressure
- Increasing respiratory distress
- Altered mental status
- Often follows trauma, cardiac surgery, or pericarditis

### Care and Management for Pericardial Tamponade

1. Activate EMS immediately — pericardiocentesis is required and is a hospital procedure
2. Oxygen: High-flow O₂
3. If trained: Administer a cautious IV fluid bolus to maintain cardiac preload while awaiting definitive care
4. **Do not give diuretics** — this will worsen tamponade
5. Position: Semi-recumbent or in position of best tolerance

## Cardiac Arrest — CPR Protocol

### When to Start CPR

Start CPR immediately if the person is:
- Unconscious — no response to tapping shoulders and shouting
- Not breathing or only gasping — gasping counts as not breathing
- Pulseless or pulse status is uncertain

Every minute without CPR reduces survival by 7–10%.

### Adult CPR Procedure

1. Confirm scene safety before approaching
2. Check responsiveness: Tap shoulders; shout "Are you okay?"
3. Call EMS (911 / 999 / 112) immediately; send someone to retrieve an AED
4. Open airway: Head-tilt chin-lift; use jaw thrust if spinal injury is suspected
5. Check breathing: Look, listen, and feel — no more than 10 seconds
6. Begin chest compressions:
   - Heel of hand on centre of chest (lower half of sternum)
   - Interlock hands; arms straight; shoulders directly over hands
   - Depth: 5–6 cm (2–2.5 inches)
   - Rate: 100–120 per minute
   - Allow full chest recoil between compressions — do not lean on the chest
7. Give 2 rescue breaths after every 30 compressions:
   - Head-tilt chin-lift; pinch nose closed; seal mouth completely
   - Blow for 1 second; watch for chest rise
   - Ratio: **30 compressions : 2 breaths**
8. AED: Attach as soon as available; follow voice prompts; resume CPR immediately after each shock with no delay
9. Continue until EMS arrives, signs of life return, or rescuer is unable to continue

### Compression-Only CPR

Acceptable when:
- Rescuer is untrained in rescue breaths
- Rescuer is unwilling to give breaths
- Adult cardiac arrest with a presumed primary cardiac cause

Rate: 100–120 compressions per minute continuously without interruption

### CPR Key Parameters — Adult vs Child

- **Compression depth:** Adult 5–6 cm; Child (1 yr to puberty) ~5 cm
- **Compression rate:** Adult and Child both 100–120/min
- **Ratio:** Adult and Child both 30:2
- **Rescue breath duration:** Adult and Child both 1 second each

### Post-Resuscitation Care

1. Breathing resumes: Place in recovery position
2. Monitor airway and breathing continuously
3. Keep warm; prevent hypothermia
4. Do not give oral fluids until fully conscious
5. Transfer to hospital immediately for post-cardiac-arrest care

## Differential Diagnosis — Chest Pain Not of Cardiac Origin

Consider and rule out the following when assessing chest pain:

- **Pulmonary Embolism (PE):** Sudden-onset difficulty breathing, sharp pleuritic chest pain worsening with inspiration, tachycardia, leg swelling or DVT
- **Pneumothorax:** Sudden unilateral chest pain, decreased breath sounds on one side; tracheal deviation indicates tension pneumothorax
- **Aortic Dissection:** Tearing or ripping pain radiating to the back, unequal blood pressure between arms, new aortic regurgitation murmur
- **Oesophageal Spasm:** Pain relieved by antacids or nitrates — may closely mimic ACS
- **Musculoskeletal:** Pain reproducible on palpation; positional in nature

## Criteria for Immediate EMS Activation — Cardiac Emergencies

Activate EMS immediately for any of the following:
- Chest pain lasting more than 5 minutes
- Chest pain accompanied by shortness of breath, sweating, or vomiting
- Pulselessness or loss of consciousness
- Pulse rate > 150 or < 40 bpm
- SpO₂ < 90% despite supplemental oxygen
- Systolic BP < 90 mmHg
