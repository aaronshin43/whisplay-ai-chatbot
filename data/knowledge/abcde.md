# ABCDE Primary Survey and SAMPLE History — WHO BEC Reference

---

## Purpose of the ABCDE Approach

The ABCDE approach provides a systematic framework to rapidly identify and treat life-threatening conditions in order of priority. Complete the initial ABCDE in the first 5 minutes. Repeat whenever a patient's condition changes or worsens — at minimum every 15 minutes.

- **A — Airway:** Check for and correct any obstruction to air movement into the lungs
- **B — Breathing:** Ensure adequate air movement into the lungs
- **C — Circulation:** Evaluate perfusion adequacy; identify life-threatening bleeding
- **D — Disability:** Assess and protect brain and spinal cord function
- **E — Exposure:** Identify all injuries and environmental threats; prevent hypothermia

A problem found at any step must be treated immediately before advancing to the next step.

---

## Scene Safety and PPE

### Scene Hazards — Check Before Approaching

- Fire, electrical wires, or chemical spills
- Road traffic crash: Is the scene closed to oncoming traffic?
- Building collapse: Is it safe to enter? Delayed collapse may follow explosions, fires, and earthquakes
- Violence: Agitated or aggressive patient — request security or police before assessment
- Infectious disease risk: Suspected flu, haemorrhagic fever, or other exposure risk

### PPE Requirements

- Wear appropriate PPE before approaching every patient — cause of illness or injury is often unknown at first contact
- Minimum: Gloves and eye protection for all patient contact
- Bodily fluid exposure: Add gown and mask
- Suspected haemorrhagic fever: Follow current local specific protective practices
- Wash hands before and after every patient contact; use alcohol gel if hand washing is not immediately available
- Between patients: Clean and disinfect all surfaces and reusable equipment

## A — Airway Assessment and Management

### Airway Assessment

- Patient talking normally: Airway is open
- Patient not talking normally: Look for chest wall movement; listen for air movement from mouth or nose
- Listen for abnormal sounds: Stridor, grunting, snoring, or hoarse/raspy voice = partial obstruction
- Stridor with swelling and/or hives = suspect anaphylaxis
- Look and listen for fluid in the airway (blood, vomit)
- Check for foreign body, abnormal swelling, or altered mental status
- Assess ability to swallow saliva — drooling indicates inability to manage secretions

### Airway Management

- Unconscious patient, not breathing normally, no trauma: Open airway with head-tilt chin-lift
- Unconscious patient, concern for trauma: Maintain cervical spine immobilisation; open airway with jaw thrust only
- Place oropharyngeal (OPA) or nasopharyngeal (NPA) airway to maintain patency as needed
- Foreign body visible: Remove carefully — do not push deeper
- Patient able to cough or make sounds: Keep calm; encourage coughing
- Patient choking (unable to cough, silent): Use age-appropriate chest thrusts, abdominal thrusts, or back blows
- Patient loses consciousness while choking: Follow relevant CPR protocol
- Secretions or vomit present: Suction if available; wipe clean; consider recovery position if ABCDE otherwise normal and no trauma suspected
- Anaphylaxis suspected (stridor, hives, swelling): Give intramuscular adrenaline; allow position of comfort; prepare for rapid transfer
- Partial obstruction: Reassess frequently — can progress to complete obstruction rapidly

## A — Airway Obstruction: Specific Conditions

### Foreign Body Obstruction

- Signs: Visible secretions or foreign body; abnormal airway sounds (stridor, snoring, gurgling); mental status changes; poor chest rise
- Management: Remove only if clearly visible; do not push deeper; use age-appropriate abdominal thrusts, chest thrusts, or back blows for complete obstruction; arrange rapid transfer for advanced airway management if obstruction cannot be cleared

### Burn-Related Airway Obstruction

- Signs: Burns to head or neck; burned nasal hairs or soot around nose or mouth; stridor; voice change; poor chest rise
- Management: Give oxygen to all patients with suspected airway burn — even without signs of hypoxia; open airway with appropriate manoeuvre; place OPA or NPA as needed; maintain cervical spine immobilisation if trauma present; arrange immediate transfer — burn airways can close very rapidly

### Anaphylaxis Airway Obstruction

- Signs: Mouth, lip, and tongue swelling; difficulty breathing with stridor and/or wheezing; rash or hives; tachycardia and hypotension; abnormal airway sounds; poor chest rise
- Management: Give intramuscular adrenaline immediately — adrenaline can wear off quickly, be prepared to repeat; establish IV access and give IV fluids; reposition airway as tolerated (sit upright if no trauma); give oxygen; arrange urgent transfer for advanced airway management if not improving

### Trauma-Related Airway Obstruction

- Signs: Neck haematoma or head/neck injuries; abnormal airway sounds; voice change; poor chest rise
- Management: Suction blood from the airway; jaw thrust only — do not use head-tilt chin-lift with trauma; place OPA as needed — do not use NPA with facial trauma; maintain cervical spine immobilisation; arrange urgent transfer for advanced airway management or surgical intervention

## B — Breathing Assessment and Management

### Breathing Assessment

- Look, listen, and feel for breathing
- Assess rate: Very fast, very slow, or very shallow
- Signs of increased work of breathing: Accessory muscle use, chest indrawing/retractions, nasal flaring, abnormal chest wall movement
- Listen for abnormal breath sounds: Wheezing, crackles
- Severe wheezing: May have silent chest — no audible breath sounds due to severe airway narrowing
- Compare breath sounds on both sides: Absent on one side = pneumothorax or haemothorax
- Absent breath sounds unilaterally with hypotension: Check for distended neck veins and tracheal shift — indicates tension pneumothorax
- Check pulse oximetry when available

### Breathing Management

- Unconscious with abnormal breathing: Start BVM ventilation; follow CPR protocol
- Not breathing adequately (too slow or too shallow): Begin BVM ventilation with oxygen — if oxygen is not immediately available, do not delay ventilation; start BVM immediately while oxygen is prepared
- Breathing fast or hypoxic: Give oxygen
- Wheezing: Give salbutamol; repeat as needed
- Anaphylaxis suspected: Give intramuscular adrenaline
- Tension pneumothorax suspected: Perform needle decompression immediately; give IV fluids and oxygen; arrange urgent transfer for chest tube placement
- Large pleural effusion or haemothorax suspected: Give oxygen; arrange immediate transfer — drainage procedure will be required

---

## B — Breathing: Specific Conditions

### Tension Pneumothorax

- Signs: Hypotension WITH difficulty breathing, PLUS any of: distended neck veins, absent breath sounds on affected side, hyperresonance on percussion on affected side, tracheal shift away from affected side
- Management: Needle decompression immediately; give oxygen and IV fluids; arrange urgent transfer for chest tube

### Opioid Overdose (Respiratory Depression)

- Signs: Slow respiratory rate, hypoxia, very small (pinpoint) pupils
- Management: Give naloxone to reverse opioid effect; monitor closely — naloxone wears off and additional doses may be needed; give oxygen

### Asthma and COPD

- Signs: Wheezing, cough, accessory muscle use; may have history of asthma, COPD, allergies, or smoking
- Management: Administer salbutamol as soon as possible; give oxygen if indicated

### Large Pleural Effusion / Haemothorax

- Signs: Decreased breath sounds on affected side; dull percussion on affected side; shock if fluid volume is large
- Management: Give oxygen; arrange immediate transfer — drainage procedure required

## C — Circulation Assessment and Management

### Circulation Assessment

- Assess perfusion: Cool and moist extremities, capillary refill > 3 seconds, low blood pressure, tachypnoea, tachycardia, absent pulses
- Check for both external AND internal bleeding:
  - Into chest or abdomen
  - From stomach or intestine (rectal or vaginal bleeding, blood in vomit)
  - From pelvic or femur fracture
  - From wounds
  - Bruising around the umbilicus or over the flanks = sign of internal bleeding
- Check for pericardial tamponade: Hypotension + distended neck veins + muffled heart sounds

### Circulation Management

- Cardiac arrest: Follow CPR protocol
- Signs of poor perfusion: Give IV fluids and oxygen
- External bleeding: Apply direct pressure; use deep wound packing, tourniquet, pelvic binder, or femur splint as indicated
- Internal bleeding or pericardial tamponade suspected: Refer urgently to a surgical centre
- Unknown cause: Consider trauma — bind pelvic fractures and splint femur fractures with compromised blood flow

---

## C — Circulation: Specific Conditions

### Shock

- Signs: Tachycardia, tachypnoea, pale and cool skin, capillary refill > 3 seconds, diaphoresis, possible hypotension, dizziness, confusion, or altered mental status
- Management: Lay patient flat if tolerated; give oxygen; control bleeding; establish IV and give IV fluids; give antibiotics if signs of infection are present; arrange urgent transfer

### Severe Haemorrhage

- Signs: Bleeding wounds; bruising around umbilicus or flanks; bleeding from rectum or vagina or in vomit; pelvic or femur fracture; decreased breath sounds on one side (haemothorax); signs of poor perfusion
- Management: Stop bleeding using direct pressure, deep wound packing, tourniquet (life-threatening bleeding only), pelvic binder, or femur splint; give IV fluids; refer for blood transfusion and surgical management as needed

### Pericardial Tamponade

- Signs: Signs of poor perfusion (tachycardia, tachypnoea, hypotension, pale and cool skin, capillary refill > 3 seconds), distended neck veins, muffled heart sounds, altered mental status
- Management: Give IV fluids to maximise cardiac filling while awaiting definitive care; refer rapidly for pericardiocentesis

## D — Disability Assessment and Management

### Disability Assessment

- Assess level of consciousness using AVPU (or GCS in trauma):
  - **A — Alert**
  - **V — Voice:** Responds to verbal stimuli
  - **P — Pain:** Responds only to painful stimuli
  - **U — Unresponsive**
- Check blood glucose in any confused or unconscious patient
- Assess pupils: Size, equality, and reactivity to light
- Check movement and sensation in all four limbs if no spinal injury concern
- Look for abnormal repetitive movements or shaking on one or both sides (seizure)

### Disability Management

- Altered mental status with no trauma: Place in recovery position
- Blood glucose < 3.5 mmol/L, or glucose testing unavailable with altered mental status: Give glucose immediately
- Active seizure: Give a benzodiazepine
- Pregnant patient with seizures: Give magnesium sulphate
- Small pupils with slow breathing: Suspect opioid overdose — give naloxone
- Unequal pupils: Suspect raised intracranial pressure — raise head of bed 30 degrees; arrange urgent transfer to neurosurgical care
- Unknown cause: Immobilise the cervical spine if trauma is possible

---

## D — Disability: Specific Conditions

### Hypoglycaemia

- Signs: Diaphoresis, altered mental status (confusion to unconsciousness), seizures, blood glucose < 3.5 mmol/L; history of diabetes, malaria, or severe infection; rapid improvement with glucose
- Management: Patient able to speak and swallow — give oral glucose; patient unable to speak or unconscious — give IV glucose; IV not available — give buccal glucose (inside of cheek)

### Raised Intracranial Pressure

- Signs: Headache, seizures, nausea and vomiting, altered mental status, unequal pupils, one-sided weakness
- Management: Raise head of bed to 30 degrees if no trauma and no hypotension; check glucose; give benzodiazepine for seizures; arrange urgent transfer to surgical centre — intracranial pressure must be reduced as rapidly as possible

### Seizure / Convulsion

- Signs of active seizure: Repetitive movements, gaze fixed to one side or alternating rhythmically, unresponsive
- Signs of recent seizure: Bitten tongue, urinary incontinence, known seizure history, confusion gradually improving over minutes to hours
- Management: Protect from falls and nearby hard or sharp objects; do not place anything in the mouth except for airway suctioning; give oxygen; check blood glucose — give glucose if < 3.5 mmol/L; give benzodiazepine and monitor closely for respiratory depression; place in recovery position if no trauma suspected; pregnant or recently postpartum patient: give magnesium sulphate

## E — Exposure Assessment and Management

### Exposure Assessment

- Examine the entire body for hidden injuries, rashes, bites, or lesions
- Hives = possible allergic reaction
- Petechial or other rashes = possible serious infection

### Exposure Management

- Snake bite suspected: Immobilise the limb; photograph the snake from a safe distance if possible and send image with the patient; give IV fluids if signs of shock; monitor closely for delayed shock or airway compromise; arrange early transfer
- Remove all constricting clothing and jewellery
- Cover the patient immediately after exposure to prevent hypothermia — acutely ill patients cannot regulate temperature normally
- Remove all wet clothing; dry the patient thoroughly
- Respect patient dignity and protect modesty throughout examination

## Vital Signs — Normal Reference Values

### Normal Adult Vital Signs

- Pulse rate: 60–100 beats per minute
- Respiratory rate: 10–20 breaths per minute — rate below 8/min is a danger sign requiring intervention
- Systolic blood pressure: > 90 mmHg

**Pulse-based systolic BP estimation (adults):**
- Carotid (neck) pulse palpable: Systolic BP ≥ 60 mmHg
- Femoral (groin) pulse palpable: Systolic BP ≥ 70 mmHg
- Radial (wrist) pulse palpable: Systolic BP ≥ 80 mmHg

### Normal Paediatric Vital Signs by Age

- Age ≤ 1 year: HR 100–160 bpm
- Age 1–3 years: HR 90–150 bpm
- Age 4–5 years: HR 80–140 bpm
- Age ≤ 2 months: RR 40–60/min
- Age 2–12 months: RR 25–50/min
- Age 1–5 years: RR 20–40/min

**Paediatric weight estimation (1–10 years):** (Age in years + 4) × 2 = weight in kg
Alternatively use PAWPER, Mercy TAPE, or Broselow tape.

Use the brachial artery (middle of upper arm) to check pulse in infants and small children.

## Paediatric ABCDE Considerations

### Paediatric Airway Differences and Management

- Children have larger tongues relative to their airway — higher obstruction risk
- Shorter, softer, more easily blocked necks and airways
- Larger head relative to body — increases risk of airway flexion causing obstruction
- High-risk airway signs in children: Excessive drooling, stridor, airway swelling, refusal to move the neck
- Positioning:
  - Place child in "sniffing" position (modified head-tilt chin-lift — slight upward tilt as if sniffing a flower)
  - Avoid over-extending or flexing the neck
  - Very small children: Place padding under the shoulders to open the airway
  - Use jaw thrust if airway is not opened by positioning
- Choking: Use age-appropriate chest thrusts, abdominal thrusts, or back blows
- Allow child to remain in position of comfort

### Paediatric Breathing Danger Signs

- Nasal flaring, head bobbing, grunting, chest indrawing/retractions = respiratory distress
- Cyanosis (blue/grey discolouration of lips, mouth, or fingertips) = hypoxia danger sign
- Chest indrawing: Lower chest wall moves inward when child breathes in — in normal breathing, entire chest and abdomen move outward on inspiration
- Silent chest (no breath sounds on auscultation) = severe respiratory distress — give salbutamol and oxygen immediately; reassess frequently
- Stridor = severe airway compromise — allow position of comfort; transfer immediately; consider IM adrenaline if transfer is not immediately possible

### Paediatric Circulation Considerations

- Children maintain normal blood pressure longer than adults when in shock — low blood pressure in a child indicates severe shock
- Monitor closely for other signs of poor perfusion: Decreased urine output, altered mental status
- IV fluid volume for children is different from adults — calculate per weight
- Malnourished children: Both fluid rate and fluid type differ from standard paediatric dosing
- Severe signs of dehydration/shock: Sunken fontanelle, poor skin pinch, lethargy, altered mental status

### Paediatric Disability Considerations

- Low blood glucose is a very common cause of altered mental status in sick children
- Check blood glucose in all children with altered mental status; administer glucose if testing is unavailable
- Always check for seizures
- Infants with altered behaviour: Always ask the caregiver — normal behaviour varies and is difficult to assess without knowing the child

### Paediatric Exposure Considerations

- Infants and children lose body temperature rapidly — hypothermia and hyperthermia both develop quickly
- Remove wet clothing; dry skin thoroughly; place infants skin-to-skin when possible
- Hypothermia: Cover the infant's head — do not obstruct the face
- Hyperthermia: Unbundle tightly wrapped infants immediately

## Paediatric Danger Signs — Require Urgent Attention

Any of the following = urgent care and immediate transfer to advanced paediatric provider:
- Signs of airway obstruction: Stridor, drooling, or inability to swallow saliva
- Increased breathing effort: Fast breathing, nasal flaring, grunting, chest indrawing, or retractions
- Cyanosis: Blue skin colour especially at lips and fingertips
- Altered mental status: Lethargy, unusual sleepiness, confusion, or disorientation
- Moves only when stimulated or no movement at all (AVPU other than Alert)
- Not feeding well; unable to drink or breastfeed
- Vomiting everything
- Seizures or convulsions
- Hypothermia (low body temperature)

## SAMPLE History

Gather SAMPLE history after completing the ABCDE assessment. Sources include the patient, family, bystanders, and prior providers.

- **S — Signs and Symptoms:** What hurts? How do you feel? Any pain, nausea, or light-headedness?
- **A — Allergies:** Known allergies to medications or other substances? What reaction occurs? Allergies may indicate anaphylaxis as the cause of acute symptoms
- **M — Medications:** Full current medication list including OTC; any recent dose changes? Relevant to treatment decisions and understanding chronic conditions
- **P — Past Medical History:** Prior conditions that may affect current illness or change management
- **L — Last Oral Intake:** Time of last oral intake; solid or liquid — full stomach increases vomiting and aspiration risk with sedation or intubation
- **E — Events:** Circumstances surrounding the injury or illness; helps establish cause, progression, and severity

---

## Handover and Disposition

- Any ABCDE intervention required: Plan immediately for handover or transfer to a higher level of care
- Complete ABCDE, then take SAMPLE history and perform condition-specific secondary examination
- Good handover to the next provider includes:
  - Brief patient identification
  - Relevant SAMPLE history elements
  - Physical examination findings
  - All interventions performed
  - Plan for next care steps and any outstanding concerns
