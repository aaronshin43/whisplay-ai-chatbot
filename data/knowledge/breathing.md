# Approach to Difficulty in Breathing (DIB) — WHO BEC Reference

## Overview of Difficulty in Breathing (DIB)

DIB ranges from subjective shortness of breath to visibly abnormal breathing or increased breathing effort. Causes include:
- Upper or lower airway obstruction (foreign body, airway spasm, swelling from allergy, infection, or injury)
- Fluid in the lung airspaces (pneumonia, pulmonary oedema)
- Air or fluid outside the lung causing collapse or compression (pneumothorax, effusion, haemothorax)
- Blood clots in the pulmonary vessels (pulmonary embolism)
- Reduced oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood (anaemia)
- Conditions that drive increased respiratory rate (toxic ingestion, diabetic ketoacidosis, anxiety)

**Initial assessment goal:** Identify reversible causes; recognise conditions requiring urgent intervention or rapid transfer.

**Acute management goal:** Keep the airway open; ensure breathing is adequate to deliver oxygen to the organs.

Always perform ABCDE first, then SAMPLE history, then secondary examination.

---

## ABCDE Key Elements for Difficulty in Breathing

### A — Airway in DIB

- DIB may result from airway swelling due to anaphylaxis or foreign body obstruction
- Stridor = serious upper airway narrowing — act immediately

### B — Breathing in DIB

- Hypotension WITH absent breath sounds on one side AND distended neck veins or tracheal shift = tension pneumothorax — treat immediately
- Wheezing = asthma or severe allergic reaction

### C — Circulation in DIB

- Shock, heart attack, heart failure, and severe infection all cause poor perfusion leading to DIB
- Check capillary refill, heart rate, and blood pressure
- Bilateral leg swelling or lung crackles = heart failure with fluid overload

### D — Disability in DIB

- Decreased level of consciousness = patient may not be able to protect the airway
- Drugs, infection, or injury can directly suppress the brain's respiratory drive
- Assess for paralysing conditions affecting breathing muscles
- Assess level of consciousness with AVPU scale

### E — Exposure in DIB

- Expose the patient fully to assess for abnormal chest wall movement and signs of trauma
- Penetrating trauma to the back, chest, underarms, or abdomen can cause lung injury and is frequently missed

## SAMPLE History for Difficulty in Breathing

### S — Signs and Symptoms

- **Sudden-onset DIB:** Suspect foreign body airway obstruction, anaphylaxis, trauma, inhalation of hot gas/smoke, acute cardiac event (heart attack, arrhythmia, valve problem)
- **Rapid or deep breathing:** Suspect poisoning, high blood acid (infection or DKA), or anxiety
- **Gradual-onset DIB:** Suspect infection, chronic fluid build-up around lung (TB, heart failure), pericardial effusion, lung cancer, or chest wall disease
- **Recurrent DIB with wheeze:** Suspect asthma or COPD
- Breathing worse when lying flat: Suspect fluid in the lungs (heart failure)
- Tongue, lip, or voice change: Suspect severe allergic reaction or airway inflammation
- Stridor (high-pitched sound on inspiration): Upper airway narrowing — suspect anaphylaxis or obstruction
- Wheeze (high-pitched sound on expiration): Lower airway narrowing — suspect asthma, COPD, heart failure, or allergic reaction
- Gurgling: Blood, mucus, or fluid in the airway
- Chest pain with DIB: Suspect heart attack, pneumothorax, pneumonia, or trauma; pain worse with deep breaths (pleuritic) = infection or pulmonary embolism
- Fever or cough: Suspect infection; cough + wheeze = asthma or COPD
- Bilateral leg swelling with DIB: Suspect heart failure; unilateral leg swelling with DIB = suspect pulmonary embolism; pregnancy is a risk factor for both

### A — Allergies

- Any medication allergies or other substance allergies? Recent insect bites or stings?
- Severe allergic reactions (to food, plants, medications, insect stings) can cause DIB from airway swelling

### M — Medications

- New medications or dose changes: New medications can trigger allergic DIB
- Accidental medication overdose can suppress or stop breathing

### P — Past Medical History

- Asthma or COPD: Prior hospitalisation or intubation = high-risk patient
- Heart or kidney disease: Risk of pulmonary oedema; heart attack can present as DIB
- TB or cancer: Risk of pericardial or pleural effusion
- Diabetes: Risk of diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) — causes fast deep breathing
- Smoking: Increases risk of asthma, COPD, lung cancer, and heart attack
- HIV: Increases risk of opportunistic lung infections

### L — Last Oral Intake

- Full stomach increases risk of vomiting and aspiration, especially if airway intervention is needed

### E — Events Surrounding Illness

- DIB started while eating or drinking: Suspect choking
- DIB with exertion and chest pain: Suspect heart attack
- Found in or near water: Suspect drowning — even small amounts of inhaled water can cause serious progressive lung damage
- Pesticide or chemical exposure: Inhaled chemicals irritate airways; some pesticides absorbed through the skin cause airway fluid build-up and breathing muscle weakness
- Recent trauma: Suspect rib fractures, pneumothorax, haemothorax, or cardiac or lung bruising

## Secondary Examination for Difficulty in Breathing

### Look

**Signs of respiratory failure:**
- Accessory muscle use and increased work of breathing
- Unable to speak in full sentences
- Unable to lie down or lean back
- Diaphoresis (excessive sweating) and mottled skin
- Confusion, irritability, or agitation
- Poor chest wall movement
- Cyanosis (blue lips and fingertips)

**Pupils:**
- Very small pupils = opioid overdose or chemical/pesticide exposure
- Unequal or abnormally shaped pupils = head injury causing abnormal breathing

**Face, nose, and mouth:**
- Cyanosis around lips or nose = low blood oxygen
- Pale inner lower eyelids = severe anaemia
- Lip, tongue, and mouth swelling = allergic reaction
- Soot around mouth or nose, burned facial hair, or facial burns = smoke inhalation and airway burns
- Bleeding or swelling = trauma

**Neck and chest:**
- Distended neck veins = heart failure, tension pneumothorax, or pericardial tamponade
- Excessive neck and intercostal muscle use = significant respiratory difficulty
- Tracheal shift to one side = tension pneumothorax or tumour
- Swelling or redness of neck = infection or trauma
- Bruising, wounds, or trauma signs on chest or neck

**Breathing rate and pattern:**
- Wheeze: Asthma, COPD, or allergic reaction — prolonged expiration due to lower airway narrowing
- Fast breathing: Dehydration, severe infection, chemical imbalance, poisoning, or anxiety
- Slow and shallow breathing: Suspect opioid overdose — confirm with very small pupils and altered mental status
- Chest wall injury with pain: Limits ability to take deep breaths
- Flail chest: Segment of chest wall moving opposite to the rest during breathing — multiple rib fractures

**Legs:**
- Bilateral lower leg swelling = heart failure
- Unilateral leg swelling with DIB = suspect pulmonary embolism

**Skin:**
- Bites = possible allergic reaction source
- Hives or rash = allergic reaction; with DIB, can indicate systemic infection
- Pallor = anaemia as cause of DIB
- Circumferential chest burns = restricts chest wall expansion and limits breathing

### Listen

**Breath sounds:**
- Stridor = partial upper airway obstruction (foreign body, mass, or swelling from trauma or infection)
- Decreased or absent breath sounds = air or fluid around the lung (pneumothorax, haemothorax, effusion), airway narrowing or blockage, or infection or tumour
- Wheeze = lower airway obstruction (asthma, COPD, allergic reaction, foreign body, or tumour)
- Crackles or crepitations = fluid in the lung airspaces

**Heart sounds:**
- Abnormal heart rhythms = poor perfusion and DIB
- Heart murmur with DIB = possible heart valve damage
- Muffled or distant heart sounds with hypotension, tachycardia, and distended neck veins = pericardial tamponade

### Feel and Percuss

**Chest wall:**
- Deformity or abnormal movement on palpation = rib fracture
- Crepitus on palpation = rib fracture or subcutaneous air (associated with pneumothorax)
- Unequal chest expansion = pneumothorax, haemothorax, or flail chest

**Percussion:**
- Hollow sounds (hyperresonance) on one side = pneumothorax
- Dull sounds on one side = fluid or blood inside the lung airspaces or between the lung and chest wall

## Causes of Difficulty in Breathing — Signs by Category

### Airway Causes

- **Foreign body in the airway:** Acute DIB; visible secretions, vomit, or foreign body; abnormal airway sounds (stridor, snoring, gurgling); coughing; drooling
- **Severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis):** Lip, tongue, and mouth swelling; stridor and/or wheeze; hives or rash; tachycardia and hypotension; exposure to known allergen
- **Airway swelling (inflammation or infection):** Stridor; hoarse voice; drooling or difficulty swallowing (indicates severe swelling); unable to lie down; possible fever
- **Airway burns:** History of chemical or fire exposure; burns to head and neck, singed nasal hairs, or soot around nose or mouth; stridor; voice change

### Lung Causes

- **Pneumonia:** Fever and cough; gradually worsening laboured breathing; pleuritic chest pain; crackles on auscultation
- **Asthma / COPD:** Wheeze; cough; accessory muscle use; tripod position (leaning forward on hands); possible history of smoking or allergies
- **Pneumothorax:** Sudden onset; decreased breath sounds on one side; hyperresonance on percussion of affected side; possible pleuritic pain; possible trauma history or rib fracture; hypotension + distended neck veins + decreased breath sounds = tension pneumothorax
- **Haemothorax:** Decreased breath sounds on affected side; dull percussion on affected side; possible trauma, cancer, or TB history; shock if large
- **Pleural effusion:** Decreased breath sounds on one or both sides; dull percussion; possible history of cancer, TB, heart disease, or kidney disease; acute or chronic DIB
- **Acute chest syndrome (sickle cell disease):** History of sickle cell disease; chest pain; fever; hypoxia

### Heart Causes

- **Heart attack:** Pressure, tightness, or crushing chest sensation; diaphoresis and mottled skin; nausea or vomiting; signs of heart failure; risk factors (smoking, heart disease, hypertension, diabetes, high cholesterol, family history)
- **Heart failure:** Worse with exertion and when lying flat; bilateral leg swelling; distended neck veins; lung crackles; possible chest pain
- **Pericardial tamponade:** Signs of poor perfusion (tachycardia, tachypnoea, hypotension, pale and cold skin, capillary refill > 3 seconds); distended neck veins; muffled heart sounds; possible dizziness, confusion, altered mental status; history of TB, trauma, malignancy, or kidney failure

### Systemic Causes

- **Anaemia:** Pale skin and pale inner lower eyelids; tachycardia; tachypnoea; history of haemorrhage, malnourishment, cancer, pregnancy, malaria, sickle cell disease, or renal failure
- **Opioid overdose:** Clinical or recreational opioid use; altered mental status; very small pupils; slow and shallow breathing
- **Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA):** Known or new diabetes history; deep or rapid breathing; frequent urination; sweet-smelling breath; high blood or urine glucose; dehydration

## Management of Difficulty in Breathing — By Condition

First: Perform ABCDE assessment and intervene for all life-threatening conditions.

Provide BVM ventilation for: any patient not breathing or not breathing adequately (too slow or too shallow); any unconscious patient with abnormal breathing; any patient with a pulse who is not breathing. Patient without pulse: Follow CPR protocol.

### Airway Inflammation or Burns
1. Keep patient calm
2. Give oxygen if possible without causing further distress
3. Seated position if fully alert and no spinal injury suspected
4. Airway burns: Arrange early intubation — airway swells rapidly and delays make intubation more difficult
5. Arrange urgent transfer

### Choking
- Use age-appropriate chest thrusts, abdominal thrusts, or back blows

### Severe Allergic Reaction (Anaphylaxis)
1. Remove the allergen if possible
2. Give intramuscular adrenaline as soon as possible
3. Give oxygen for severe cases

### Asthma / COPD
1. Administer salbutamol as soon as possible
2. Give oxygen if indicated

### Fever and Suspected Infection
1. Give antibiotics as soon as possible
2. Signs of poor perfusion present: Give IV fluids cautiously to avoid fluid overload

### Heart Attack
1. Give aspirin
2. Give oxygen if shock or DIB is present — oxygen is not required for all heart attack patients
3. Patient has prescribed nitroglycerine and perfusion is adequate: Assist in taking it

### Chronic Severe Anaemia
1. Give IV fluids slowly
2. Check lungs frequently for crackles (fluid overload indicator)
3. Arrange transfer for blood transfusion

### Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)
1. Give IV fluids
2. Arrange urgent transfer to an advanced provider — DKA is a critical emergency

### Opioid Overdose
1. Support breathing with BVM as needed
2. Give naloxone

### Pleural Effusion or Haemothorax
1. Give oxygen
2. Arrange immediate transfer — chest tube or drainage procedure will be required

### Trauma with DIB
1. Give oxygen to all trauma patients with DIB
2. Suspected pericardial tamponade or tension pneumothorax: Give IV fluids to fill the heart
3. Confirmed tension pneumothorax: Perform needle decompression immediately
4. Sucking chest wound: Apply three-sided dressing
5. Needle decompression performed or three-sided dressing applied: Arrange urgent transfer for chest tube

### Acute Chest Syndrome (Sickle Cell Disease)
1. Give oxygen, IV fluids, and antibiotics
2. Arrange transfer for advanced management if needed

## Paediatric Considerations for Difficulty in Breathing

### Danger Signs in Children with DIB

Any of the following = urgent care and immediate transfer:
- Signs of airway obstruction: Drooling, unable to swallow saliva, or stridor
- Increased breathing effort: Fast breathing, nasal flaring, grunting, chest indrawing, or retractions
- Cyanosis: Blue colouration at lips and fingertips
- Altered mental status: Lethargy, unusual sleepiness, or agitation
- Poor feeding or inability to drink
- Vomiting everything
- Seizures or convulsions
- Hypothermia (low body temperature)

### Paediatric-Specific Considerations

- Wheeze in children: Caused by viral infection, asthma, or inhaled foreign body blocking the airway
- Stridor in children: Caused by a foreign object in the upper airway OR airway swelling
- Pneumonia in children: Rapid breathing may be the only sign — do not miss this
- Rapid breathing in children: Also indicates diabetic crisis (DKA) — may be the first presentation of diabetes in a child

## Disposition Considerations for DIB

- Salbutamol (inhaled): Effect lasts approximately 3 hours — monitor closely and re-dose as needed
- Adrenaline given for anaphylaxis: Reaction can return when adrenaline wears off — monitor continuously
- Naloxone: Lasts approximately 1 hour — most opioids last longer; repeat doses may be needed
- Drowning: Late breathing problems can develop several hours after the event — observe closely
- Any patient who may need definitive airway placement: Never leave unmonitored during transfer — confirm a new provider is monitoring before leaving
- Any patient who may need assisted ventilation: Arrange transfer as early as possible
