# Heat-Related Emergencies — Wilderness First Aid Reference

**Applies to:** All ages; field settings without rapid EMS access

---

## Heat Cramps

Heat cramps are muscle cramps caused by fluid and electrolyte loss during exertion in heat.

### Care and Management for Heat Cramps

1. Move the person to a cool place to rest
2. Give a carbohydrate-electrolyte solution (commercial sports drink or fruit juice); water is acceptable if unavailable
3. Lightly stretch the affected muscle and gently massage the area
4. Do not give salt tablets
5. Cramps resolved: Person may resume activity — monitor for further signs of heat-related illness

---

## Heat Exhaustion

Heat exhaustion results from heat stress combined with water and electrolyte loss (primarily through sweat) and inadequate hydration, typically during exercise.

### Signs and Symptoms of Heat Exhaustion

- Sweating
- Exhaustion
- Pale, sweaty, or flushed skin
- Headache
- Nausea and/or vomiting
- Thirst and decreased urine output — urine is yellowish
- Dizziness on standing quickly (orthostatic)
- Elevated heart rate and respiratory rate
- Slightly elevated core temperature (not always present)

### Care and Management for Heat Exhaustion

1. Stop all activity; have the person rest in a cool, shady place
2. Replace lost fluids and salt — give small amounts of carbohydrate-electrolyte solution (sports drink or fruit juice) frequently; do not give salt tablets (too concentrated)
3. Loosen or remove as much clothing as possible
4. Wet down the skin and fan the person continuously to increase evaporative cooling
5. Person drowsy: Allow to sleep
6. Person feels better: May resume normal activity
7. Monitor for recurrence — recovery may take up to 24 hours

**Critical distinction from hyponatremia:** Heat exhaustion produces yellowish urine and thirst. Hyponatremia produces clear urine with no thirst — do not treat hyponatremia with water (see Hyponatremia section).

## Heat Stroke

Heat stroke is a life-threatening emergency. The body produces heat faster than it can be shed, typically during over-exertion and/or severe dehydration. Rapid cooling is the only way to save the person.

### Signs and Symptoms of Heat Stroke

- Core temperature ≥ 105°F (oral temperature ≥ 104°F)
- Disorientation and bizarre personality changes
- Skin that is hot and dry, or hot and moist
- Elevated heart rate and respiratory rate
- Headache
- Seizures

### Care and Management for Heat Stroke

1. Move the person immediately to a cooler environment
2. Remove all heat-retaining clothing
3. Large water source available: Immerse the person up to the neck in cold water until mental status returns to normal
4. Immersion not possible: Drench the person with cold water — concentrate on head and neck; apply cold packs to neck, armpits, groin, hands, and feet; fan continuously to maximise evaporation
5. Monitor closely — stop cooling efforts when normal mental status returns
6. Person alert and able to swallow: Give cool water or sports drink
7. Do not give fever-reducing drugs (e.g., paracetamol, ibuprofen, aspirin)
8. Person must see a healthcare provider as soon as possible — even if they appear to have fully recovered
9. Monitor carefully during evacuation — relapses are common

### Evacuation Criteria for Heat Stroke

- Altered mental status or seizure caused by heat stroke: Evacuate rapidly (GO FAST)

## Hyponatremia (Water Intoxication / Low Blood Sodium)

Hyponatremia occurs when blood sodium falls too low to maintain normal body function — typically from drinking excessive water without eating. Untreated hyponatremia progresses to seizures, coma, and death.

### Signs and Symptoms of Hyponatremia

- Headache
- Weakness and fatigue
- Light-headedness
- Muscle cramps
- Nausea, with or without vomiting
- Sweaty skin
- Clear urine — person has urinated recently
- Person reports drinking large amounts of water
- Person denies being thirsty
- Normal core or oral temperature
- Normal or slightly elevated pulse and breathing rate
- Increasing anxiety
- Altered mental status — disorientation, irritability, combativeness (severe cases = water intoxication)

### Distinguishing Hyponatremia from Heat Exhaustion

- **Heat exhaustion:** Yellowish urine, urinating infrequently (every 6–8 hours), person is thirsty
- **Hyponatremia:** Clear urine, urinated recently, person has been drinking large amounts of water, person denies thirst

### Care and Management for Hyponatremia

1. Have the person rest in shade with **no fluid intake**
2. Give gradual intake of salty foods to allow kidneys to re-establish sodium balance
3. Resolution confirmed when: Person develops hunger and thirst simultaneously with normal urine output
4. **Do not give water** — treating hyponatremia as heat exhaustion and giving water will worsen the condition and cause harm

### Evacuation Criteria for Hyponatremia

- Altered mental status present: Evacuate slowly (GO SLOW)
- Full recovery achieved in the field: Evacuation not necessary

---

## Evacuation Criteria Summary — Heat-Related Emergencies

- Heat stroke with altered mental status or seizure: Evacuate rapidly (GO FAST)
- Heat exhaustion not fully resolving: Evacuate slowly (GO SLOW)
- Mild hyponatremia not fully resolving: Evacuate slowly (GO SLOW)
- Hyponatremia with altered mental status: Evacuate slowly (GO SLOW)
