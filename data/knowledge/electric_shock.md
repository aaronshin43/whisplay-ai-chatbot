# Electric Shock — First Aid Response

## Scene Safety — Critical First Priority

### Do NOT Touch the Person Until the Power Is Off

**Electrical current can pass through the rescuer.** You must ensure the scene is safe before approaching.

### High-Voltage Sources (Power Lines, Industrial Equipment)

- Do NOT approach within 10 metres of a downed power line.
- Call emergency services and keep bystanders away.
- Wait for trained electrical workers to cut power.
- Do NOT attempt rescue until power is confirmed off.

### Low-Voltage Sources (Household Current, Extension Cords)

1. **Turn off the power** at the circuit breaker or fuse box.
2. If you cannot turn off the power: unplug the device at the wall.
3. If the plug or switch cannot be reached safely, use a **dry, non-conducting object** (dry wooden stick, dry plastic chair, dry rubber mat) to push the person away from the source.
4. Do **NOT use anything metal** or anything wet.
5. Do **NOT touch the person's skin** until contact with the electrical source is broken.

---

## First Aid Management for Electric Shock

### After the Scene Is Safe

### Immediate Assessment

1. Call emergency services (911/999/112) immediately.
2. Check if the person is conscious and breathing.
3. Do not move the person unnecessarily — electrical injuries may involve spinal cord damage.

### If Not Breathing — Begin CPR

4. If the person is not breathing, begin CPR immediately.
5. Give 30 chest compressions followed by 2 rescue breaths.
6. Continue until emergency services arrive or the person breathes normally.
7. Use an AED (automated external defibrillator) if available — electric shock frequently causes cardiac arrhythmia or cardiac arrest.

### If Breathing — Treat for Shock

8. Keep the person still and calm.
9. Lay the person flat; elevate legs slightly unless head, neck, or spinal injury is suspected.
10. Keep the person warm with a blanket.
11. Do NOT give food or water.

## Burns from Electric Shock

Electric shock causes entry and exit burns where current entered and exited the body.

### Internal Burns (Invisible)

- Electrical current damages tissues along its path through the body.
- Internal burns are often more severe than visible surface burns.
- Assume internal injury even if external burns appear minor.

### External Burns

- Cool electrical burns with cool running water for 10–20 minutes.
- Do NOT use ice.
- Cover loosely with a clean, dry dressing.
- Do NOT remove clothing stuck to burns.

---

## When to Call Emergency Services

**Always call 911/999/112 after electric shock, even if the person appears fine.**

Electric shock can cause:
- Delayed cardiac arrhythmia
- Hidden internal burns
- Neurological damage
- Muscle breakdown (rhabdomyolysis) requiring IV fluids

### Specific Criteria

- High-voltage exposure (>1000V) — always transport to hospital
- Unconsciousness, even briefly
- Any cardiac symptoms (chest pain, palpitations, irregular heartbeat)
- Any burns
- Pregnancy
- Neurological symptoms (confusion, weakness, numbness)

---

## Do NOT

- Do NOT touch the person while they are still in contact with the electrical source
- Do NOT move the person unless necessary for safety
- Do NOT apply ice to electrical burns
- Do NOT give food or water if the person is confused or has burns
- Do NOT assume the person is fine after a "minor" shock — delayed effects are common
