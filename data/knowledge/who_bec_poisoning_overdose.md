## ⚠️ SEIZURE — Absolute Contraindications (DO NOT DO)

> **During active convulsions:**
> 1. Do NOT restrain or hold down the person — allow movement to prevent fractures.
> 2. Do NOT put anything in the mouth — risk of broken teeth, jaw injury, and aspiration.
> 3. Do NOT attempt pulse or breathing checks while convulsing — wait until shaking stops.


## General Approach to Poisoning

### Primary Survey — ABCDE

Apply standard ABCDE approach. Poisoning affects multiple systems simultaneously.

**Airway:** Most common cause of death in poisoning is airway obstruction from altered consciousness. Position patient; prepare suction.

**Breathing:** Respiratory depression (slow, shallow, or absent breathing) requires immediate assisted ventilation.

**Circulation:** Toxins cause arrhythmias, hypotension, shock. Establish IV access if trained.

**Disability:** Assess GCS/AVPU. Altered mental status (AMS) in poisoning — check glucose first.

**Exposure:** Remove contaminated clothing; decontaminate skin if chemical exposure.

### History — Key Questions

- **What** was taken? (substance name, concentration, formulation)
- **How much?** (dose, number of tablets/mL)
- **When?** (time of ingestion/exposure)
- **Route?** (ingested, inhaled, injected, dermal)
- **Any treatment** already given?
- Alcohol or other drugs co-ingested?
- Medical history; prescribed medications

### DO NOT

- Do NOT induce vomiting (increased risk of aspiration, chemical burns)
- Do NOT give milk or water to "dilute" caustic agents without guidance
- Do NOT delay transport for decontamination in unstable patients

## Opioid Overdose

### Recognition

**Classic triad:**
1. **Unconsciousness / altered mental status** (unresponsive)
2. **Respiratory depression** (slow < 12/min, shallow, or apnoeic)
3. **Pinpoint pupils** (miosis)

**Additional signs:**
- Cyanosis (blue lips/fingertips)
- Slow, snoring or gurgling respirations
- Bradycardia, hypotension
- Cool, clammy skin
- History of opioid use (heroin, morphine, codeine, fentanyl, oxycodone, tramadol)

### Management

1. **Airway:** Open and maintain airway (head-tilt chin-lift, recovery position if breathing).
2. **Breathing:** If respiratory rate < 12/min or apnoeic — give rescue breaths or BVM ventilation.
3. **Naloxone (opioid antidote):**
   - Dose: **0.4–2 mg IV, IM, or intranasal**
   - Repeat every 2–3 minutes as needed up to 10 mg
   - Duration of naloxone (30–90 min) is shorter than most opioids — patient may re-narcotize; monitor closely
   - Nasally: 1 mg per nostril (if 2 mg/2 mL preparation)
4. **Recovery position:** Once breathing is adequate and airway secure.
5. **Activate EMS** — all opioid overdoses require hospital evaluation.
6. **Monitor:** Respiratory rate, SpO₂, GCS every 5 minutes.

### Caution

- Naloxone may precipitate acute opioid withdrawal (agitation, hypertension, vomiting) — not life-threatening but uncomfortable
- In opioid-dependent patients, use lowest effective dose to restore breathing without precipitating full withdrawal

## Organophosphate / Pesticide Poisoning

### Recognition

Organophosphates (OP) and carbamates inhibit acetylcholinesterase → excess acetylcholine at all receptors.

**SLUDGE syndrome (muscarinic excess):**
- **S**alivation (excessive drooling)
- **L**acrimation (tearing)
- **U**rination (incontinence)
- **D**efecation / Diarrhoea
- **G**astrointestinal distress (nausea, vomiting, cramps)
- **E**mesis (vomiting)

**Additional signs:**
- Miosis (small pupils), blurred vision
- Bronchospasm, excessive secretions, pulmonary oedema
- Bradycardia
- Nicotinic effects: muscle fasciculations, weakness, paralysis
- Central: AMS, seizures, coma
- Common in agricultural settings; may occur after pesticide spray

### Management

1. **Decontaminate:** Remove and bag contaminated clothing; irrigate skin and eyes with large amounts of water (protect yourself — wear gloves).
2. **Airway/Breathing:** Suction secretions aggressively; assisted ventilation if needed.
3. **Atropine (antidote):**
   - 2–4 mg IV/IM every 5–10 minutes until secretions dry (target: dry airways, not pupil size)
   - May need very large cumulative doses (20–100 mg in severe cases)
4. **Pralidoxime (2-PAM):** Reactivates cholinesterase if given early (< 24–48 h); 1–2 g IV over 15–30 min if available.
5. **Seizures:** Diazepam 5–10 mg IV/IM.
6. **Activate EMS** — ICU-level care required for severe cases.
7. **Decontaminate the rescuer** — OP poisoning can be transferred to rescuers through skin contact.

## Carbon Monoxide (CO) Poisoning

### Recognition

CO binds haemoglobin (carboxyhaemoglobin) with 200× affinity of O₂ → tissue hypoxia despite normal-appearing SpO₂.

**Clinical features (correlate with CO level):**
- Mild: Headache (frontal), nausea, dizziness, fatigue
- Moderate: Confusion, ataxia, chest pain, vomiting
- Severe: Altered consciousness, seizures, coma, cardiac arrhythmias

**Key clue:** Multiple people (or pets) in enclosed space affected simultaneously (faulty heater, generator, fire).

**Pulse oximetry is UNRELIABLE** — may show normal SpO₂ despite severe CO poisoning (SpO₂ measures oxy + carboxyhaemoglobin).

### Management

1. **Remove from exposure** — ensure scene safety first (CO is colourless, odourless; ventilate space or use SCBA).
2. **100% Oxygen** via tight-fitting non-rebreather mask — reduces CO half-life from 5 h to ~60–90 min.
3. **High-flow O₂ for at least 4–6 hours** even if patient feels better.
4. **Hyperbaric oxygen (HBO):** Indicated for severe cases (LOC, cardiac involvement, neurological deficits, pregnant patients) — hospital decision.
5. **Activate EMS** — all symptomatic CO exposures require hospital evaluation.
6. **Do NOT use pulse oximetry** to guide O₂ therapy in CO poisoning.
7. If unconscious: CPR if needed; airway management; transport urgently.

## Alcohol (Ethanol) Withdrawal

### Recognition

Occurs 6–72 hours after stopping heavy, prolonged alcohol use.

**Progression:**
- **6–24 h:** Tremor, anxiety, sweating, tachycardia, hypertension, nausea
- **12–24 h:** Alcoholic hallucinosis (visual/auditory hallucinations; clear sensorium)
- **24–48 h:** Withdrawal seizures (generalized tonic-clonic)
- **48–72 h:** Delirium tremens (DTs) — confusion, agitation, hyperthermia, autonomic instability; **life-threatening**

### Management

1. **Benzodiazepines** (diazepam 5–10 mg IV/IM; lorazepam; chlordiazepoxide) — first-line for withdrawal seizures and DTs.
2. **Thiamine 100 mg IV/IM** before any glucose (prevents Wernicke's encephalopathy).
3. **Glucose** if hypoglycaemic.
4. **Hydration:** IV fluids; electrolyte replacement (K⁺, Mg²⁺ often depleted).
5. **Seizures:** Benzodiazepines; phenobarbital if refractory.
6. **Activate EMS** — DTs require ICU admission.
7. **Do NOT** abruptly restrict fluids or leave patient unmonitored.

## General Poisoning — Activated Charcoal

### When to Consider

- Ingested toxic substance within **1 hour** of presentation
- Patient is conscious and able to protect airway
- Substance is adsorbed by charcoal (most drugs; NOT metals, alcohols, acids/alkalis, cyanide)

### Dose

- Adult: **50 g** in 200–400 mL water (orally or via NGT)
- Child: **1 g/kg**

### Contraindications

- Altered mental status / unprotected airway
- Caustic (acid/alkali) ingestion
- Hydrocarbon ingestion
- Bowel obstruction

## Emergency Signals — Activate EMS Immediately

- Unconsciousness or altered mental status after ingestion
- Respiratory rate < 12/min or apnoea
- Seizures
- Cardiac arrhythmia or chest pain
- Severe agitation or psychosis
- Cyanosis
- Any unknown substance ingested by child
