# Poisoning and Overdose — WHO BEC Reference

---

## General Approach to Poisoning

### ABCDE in Poisoning

- **Airway:** Airway obstruction from altered consciousness is the most common cause of death in poisoning — position the patient and prepare suction
- **Breathing:** Respiratory depression (slow, shallow, or absent breathing) requires immediate assisted ventilation
- **Circulation:** Toxins cause arrhythmias, hypotension, and shock — establish IV access if trained
- **Disability:** Assess GCS or AVPU; check blood glucose first in any patient with AMS from poisoning
- **Exposure:** Remove contaminated clothing; decontaminate skin if chemical exposure

### Key History Questions for Poisoning

- **What** was taken? (substance name, concentration, formulation)
- **How much?** (dose, number of tablets or mL)
- **When?** (time of ingestion or exposure)
- **Route?** (ingested, inhaled, injected, or dermal)
- **Any treatment** already given?
- Alcohol or other substances co-ingested?
- Medical history and prescribed medications

### General Poisoning — Do Not

- Do NOT induce vomiting — increases aspiration risk and can cause chemical burns
- Do NOT give milk or water to dilute caustic agents without specific guidance
- Do NOT delay transport for decontamination in an unstable patient

## Opioid Overdose

### Signs and Symptoms of Opioid Overdose — Classic Triad

1. Unconsciousness or altered mental status (unresponsive)
2. Respiratory depression — rate < 12/min, shallow, or apnoeic
3. Pinpoint pupils (miosis)

### Additional Signs of Opioid Overdose

- Cyanosis (blue lips or fingertips)
- Slow, snoring, or gurgling respirations
- Bradycardia and hypotension
- Cool, clammy skin
- History of opioid use: heroin, morphine, codeine, fentanyl, oxycodone, tramadol

### Care and Management for Opioid Overdose

1. Open and maintain the airway — head-tilt chin-lift; recovery position if the patient is breathing
2. Respiratory rate < 12/min or apnoeic: Give rescue breaths or BVM ventilation immediately
3. Administer **naloxone (opioid antidote):**
   - Dose: 0.4–2 mg IV, IM, or intranasal
   - Repeat every 2–3 minutes as needed up to 10 mg total
   - Intranasal: 1 mg per nostril (using a 2 mg/2 mL preparation)
   - Duration of naloxone is 30–90 minutes — shorter than most opioids; patient may re-narcotise; monitor continuously
4. Breathing restored and airway secure: Place in recovery position
5. Activate EMS — all opioid overdoses require hospital evaluation
6. Monitor respiratory rate, SpO₂, and GCS every 5 minutes

### Caution with Naloxone

- Naloxone may precipitate acute opioid withdrawal (agitation, hypertension, vomiting) — uncomfortable but not life-threatening
- Opioid-dependent patients: Use the lowest effective dose to restore breathing without triggering full withdrawal

## Organophosphate and Pesticide Poisoning

Organophosphates (OP) and carbamates inhibit acetylcholinesterase → excess acetylcholine accumulates at all receptors.

### Signs and Symptoms of Organophosphate Poisoning — SLUDGE Syndrome (Muscarinic Excess)

- **S** — Salivation (excessive drooling)
- **L** — Lacrimation (tearing)
- **U** — Urination (incontinence)
- **D** — Defecation or diarrhoea
- **G** — Gastrointestinal distress (nausea, vomiting, cramps)
- **E** — Emesis (vomiting)

### Additional Signs of Organophosphate Poisoning

- Miosis (small pupils) and blurred vision
- Bronchospasm, excessive secretions, pulmonary oedema
- Bradycardia
- Nicotinic effects: muscle fasciculations, weakness, and paralysis
- Central effects: AMS, seizures, coma
- Common in agricultural settings; may occur after pesticide spraying

### Care and Management for Organophosphate Poisoning

1. **Decontaminate:** Remove and bag all contaminated clothing; irrigate skin and eyes with large amounts of water — wear gloves to protect yourself; OP can be transferred to rescuers through skin contact
2. **Airway and Breathing:** Suction secretions aggressively; provide assisted ventilation if needed
3. Administer **atropine (antidote):**
   - 2–4 mg IV or IM every 5–10 minutes until secretions dry (endpoint = dry airways, not pupil size)
   - Severe cases may require very large cumulative doses (20–100 mg total)
4. Administer **pralidoxime (2-PAM)** if available and given within 24–48 hours of exposure:
   - 1–2 g IV over 15–30 minutes — reactivates acetylcholinesterase
5. Seizures: Give diazepam 5–10 mg IV or IM
6. Activate EMS — severe cases require ICU-level care

## Carbon Monoxide (CO) Poisoning

CO binds haemoglobin with 200× the affinity of oxygen, forming carboxyhaemoglobin → tissue hypoxia despite apparently normal SpO₂.

**Critical warning:** Pulse oximetry is UNRELIABLE in CO poisoning — it cannot distinguish oxyhaemoglobin from carboxyhaemoglobin and may show falsely normal readings. Do not use SpO₂ to guide oxygen therapy.

### Signs and Symptoms of CO Poisoning by Severity

- **Mild:** Frontal headache, nausea, dizziness, fatigue
- **Moderate:** Confusion, ataxia, chest pain, vomiting
- **Severe:** Altered consciousness, seizures, coma, cardiac arrhythmias

**Key diagnostic clue:** Multiple people (or pets) in an enclosed space affected simultaneously — suspect faulty heater, indoor generator, or fire.

### Care and Management for CO Poisoning

1. Remove from exposure — confirm scene safety first; CO is colourless and odourless; ventilate the space or use SCBA before entering
2. Apply 100% oxygen via tight-fitting non-rebreather mask — reduces CO half-life from approximately 5 hours to 60–90 minutes
3. Continue high-flow oxygen for at least 4–6 hours — even if the patient feels better
4. Activate EMS — all symptomatic CO exposures require hospital evaluation
5. Severe cases (loss of consciousness, cardiac involvement, neurological deficits, or pregnant patient): Hyperbaric oxygen (HBO) therapy is indicated — this is a hospital-level decision
6. Unconscious patient: Manage airway; perform CPR if needed; transport urgently
7. Do not use pulse oximetry to guide oxygen therapy in CO poisoning

## Alcohol (Ethanol) Withdrawal

Alcohol withdrawal occurs 6–72 hours after stopping heavy, prolonged alcohol use.

### Signs and Symptoms of Alcohol Withdrawal — Progression by Time

- **6–24 hours:** Tremor, anxiety, sweating, tachycardia, hypertension, nausea
- **12–24 hours:** Alcoholic hallucinosis — visual or auditory hallucinations with a clear sensorium
- **24–48 hours:** Withdrawal seizures (generalised tonic-clonic)
- **48–72 hours:** Delirium tremens (DTs) — confusion, agitation, hyperthermia, autonomic instability — **life-threatening**

### Care and Management for Alcohol Withdrawal

1. Give **thiamine 100 mg IV or IM before any glucose** — prevents Wernicke's encephalopathy
2. Give **benzodiazepines** (diazepam 5–10 mg IV or IM; lorazepam; or chlordiazepoxide) — first-line treatment for withdrawal seizures and DTs
3. Give glucose if hypoglycaemic
4. Give IV fluids for hydration; replace electrolytes — potassium (K⁺) and magnesium (Mg²⁺) are commonly depleted
5. Refractory seizures: Give phenobarbital
6. Activate EMS — delirium tremens requires ICU admission
7. Do not abruptly restrict fluids; do not leave the patient unmonitored

## Activated Charcoal for Ingested Poisons

### When to Consider Activated Charcoal

- Toxic substance ingested within **1 hour** of presentation
- Patient is fully conscious and able to protect the airway
- Substance is adsorbed by charcoal: most drugs are adsorbed — NOT metals, alcohols, acids or alkalis, or cyanide

### Activated Charcoal Dosing

- Adult: 50 g in 200–400 mL water (give orally or via nasogastric tube)
- Child: 1 g/kg

### Contraindications to Activated Charcoal

- Altered mental status or unprotected airway
- Caustic (acid or alkali) ingestion
- Hydrocarbon ingestion
- Bowel obstruction

## Criteria for Immediate EMS Activation — Poisoning and Overdose

Activate EMS immediately for any of the following:
- Unconsciousness or altered mental status after ingestion
- Respiratory rate < 12/min or apnoea
- Seizures
- Cardiac arrhythmia or chest pain
- Severe agitation or psychosis
- Cyanosis
- Any unknown substance ingested by a child
