# Bites, Stings, and Allergic Reactions

## Allergic Reactions & Anaphylaxis

### Non-Life-Threatening Allergic Reaction
**Signs:** Hives, itching, localised redness, nausea or mild GI upset — no breathing difficulty or shock.
**Care:** Remove or avoid the allergen (e.g., remove stinger). Assist in self-administering antihistamine if available and able to swallow.

### Anaphylaxis (Severe Allergic Reaction)
**Signs:** Life-threatening. Skin symptoms (hives, swelling) PLUS breathing difficulty OR shock (pale, cool, sweaty, lightheaded). Known allergen exposure causing difficulty breathing or circulatory collapse.
**Care:**
1. Check for medical alert ID.
2. Remove or avoid further exposure to the allergen.
3. Assist with Epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen®): inject straight into the outer mid-thigh, hold for 10 seconds.
4. Lay the person flat with legs elevated (unless breathing is easier sitting up).
5. Call emergency services immediately — effects of epinephrine last only 15–20 minutes.
6. A second dose may be given after 5–15 minutes if no improvement.
**Evacuation:** Evacuate immediately (GO FAST) after any epinephrine use — symptoms can return.

## Venomous Snakebites

### Snakebite Signs and Care
**Signs:** Fang marks, localised pain and swelling. Nausea/tingling (moderate), shock/coma/tissue necrosis (severe).
**Care:**
1. Keep the person calm and still. Do NOT let them walk.
2. Keep the bitten extremity at or slightly below heart level.
3. Remove rings, watches, and tight items immediately before swelling begins.
4. **Neurotoxic/Systemic venom:** Apply a broad pressure immobilisation bandage upward from the bite; keep firm but do NOT cut off distal pulses. Splint the limb.
5. **Cytotoxic/Local venom:** Do NOT apply a pressure bandage.
6. Gently wash the site.
7. **Prohibited:** DO NOT cut the wound, apply ice, suck the venom, or apply a tourniquet.
8. Transport urgently to hospital — antivenom may be required.

## Insect & Arthropod Bites

### Tick Removal
Remove immediately by grasping close to the skin with fine-tipped tweezers and pulling slowly and steadily — do NOT twist or jerk. Wash the site with soap and water. If flu-like symptoms (fever, rash, muscle aches) develop within 2 weeks, seek medical evaluation.

### Bee and Wasp Stings
Scrape the stinger away with a card or fingernail — do NOT use tweezers (squeezing the venom sac injects more venom). Wash the site. Apply a cold pack to reduce pain and swelling. Monitor for signs of anaphylaxis for at least 30 minutes.

### Spider and Scorpion Bites
Wash the site. Apply wound gel or a clean dressing. Apply a cold pack. Use antivenin if available and trained to do so. Call emergency services for severe pain, breathing difficulty, or systemic symptoms.

## Animal & Marine Bites and Stings

### Animal Bites
Control serious bleeding first with direct pressure. Wash minor wounds thoroughly with soap and water and irrigate heavily. Leave serious wounds for a medical facility — do not attempt to close them. Monitor for infection. Consider rabies risk — report to public health if applicable.

### Jellyfish and Marine Stings
- **Jellyfish:** Irrigate with vinegar for at least 30 seconds (or baking soda slurry if vinegar unavailable). After stinging cells are neutralised, immerse in hot (non-scalding) water.
- **Portuguese Man-of-War:** Use ocean water to rinse — NOT vinegar.
- **Stingray:** Flush wound with water; soak in non-scalding hot water until pain resolves. Immobilise the affected limb.

## Poisonous Plant Exposure
Wash affected skin immediately with soap and water — wear gloves to avoid spreading urushiol. Apply baking soda paste or soothing lotion (e.g., calamine) to rash. Wash all exposed clothing thoroughly.

## Evacuation Criteria
- Evacuate rapidly (GO FAST) for: anaphylaxis or severe allergic reaction, severe reaction to insect or marine sting, serious animal bite or suspected rabies exposure, and any venomous snakebite.
