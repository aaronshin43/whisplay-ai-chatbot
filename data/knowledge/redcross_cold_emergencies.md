# Cold-Related Emergencies and Confined Space Rescue — Wilderness First Aid Reference

**Applies to:** All ages; field settings without rapid EMS access

---

## Hypothermia

Hypothermia is a lowering of the body's core temperature to the point where normal brain and/or muscle function is impaired. It ranges from mild to life-threatening.

---

### Signs and Symptoms of Mild Hypothermia

- Shivering
- The "Umbles":
  - Fumbles — inability to perform complex tasks
  - Grumbles — confusion, apathy, and sluggish thinking
  - Mumbles — slurred speech
  - Stumbles — altered gait
- Person is able to eat and talk
- Person complains of feeling cold

### Signs and Symptoms of Moderate Hypothermia

- Worsening of all four "Umbles"
- Uncontrollable, violent shivering
- Confusion or unusual behaviour
- Impaired judgment

### Care and Management for Mild to Moderate Hypothermia

1. Move the person out of wind and cold into shelter — even waterproof, windproof clothing provides benefit
2. Cover all exposed skin including head and neck
3. Replace all wet clothing with dry clothing
4. Person able to eat: Give simple carbohydrates (candy, juice)
5. Person able to drink: Give warm, sweet fluids (e.g., warm gelatin dessert); give cold fluids if warm fluids are unavailable
6. Do not give caffeine or alcohol
7. Person unable to exercise muscles easily — apply all of the following to maintain warmth:
   - Insulate from the ground
   - Bundle in dry insulation
   - Have person snuggle with warm people in a sleeping bag — wake periodically if the person falls asleep
   - Place hot water bottle or chemical heat packs near the heart, in the armpits, and in the groin — not directly against bare skin
8. Wait until the person returns to normal and can exercise muscles before resuming movement
9. Person still able to exercise easily: Activity may continue after initial care

---

### Signs and Symptoms of Severe Hypothermia

- Shivering stops — critical warning sign
- Increasing muscle rigidity
- Worsening mental state: Stupor progressing to coma
- Pulse and breathing become very slow and difficult to detect (but are still present)
- Semi-consciousness or unconsciousness
- Person stops complaining
- Core temperature below 90°F (32°C)

### Care and Management for Severe Hypothermia

1. Handle the person with extreme gentleness — rough movement can trigger cardiac arrest in a cold heart
2. Breathing undetectable: Perform CPR for at least 3 minutes before any movement
3. Apply all mild-to-moderate hypothermia care steps to prevent further heat loss
4. Create a **hypothermia wrap** vapour barrier:
   - Wrap the person in a tent fly, plastic sheet, garbage bags, or equivalent material to trap remaining body heat
   - The wrap must form a cocoon — open only at the mouth and nose
5. Do not force food or drink
6. Do not assume death — care for severe hypothermia even if the person appears dead; a person is not confirmed dead unless re-warmed and still unresponsive to all care
7. Call for help immediately
8. Evacuate gently — do not evacuate if gentle transport is not possible

**Critical warning:** Rewarming too quickly or moving the person too much sends cold blood to the core, dropping body temperature further — this can be fatal as the cold heart cannot function properly.

### Evacuation Criteria for Hypothermia

- Recovered from mild to moderate hypothermia with return to normal: May remain in the field
- Severe hypothermia: Evacuate rapidly (GO FAST) with extreme gentleness

## Frostbite

Frostbite is the freezing of body tissue exposed to cold. Severity depends on air temperature, duration of exposure, and wind. Frostbite can result in loss of fingers, hands, arms, toes, feet, or legs.

### Signs and Symptoms of Frostbite

- Numbness or absent sensation in the affected area
- Skin appears waxy, feels cold to touch, or is discoloured (flushed, white, yellow, or blue)
- Severe cases: Blisters form; affected part turns black with signs of deep tissue damage

### Care and Management for Frostbite

1. Handle the affected area gently
2. Never rub the affected area — rubbing causes further soft tissue damage
3. Protect from further cold exposure; treat concurrently for hypothermia
4. Do not attempt rewarming if there is any chance of re-freezing or if help is more than 2 hours away
5. Minor frostbite: Rapidly rewarm using skin-to-skin contact (e.g., a warm hand)
6. Serious frostbite in a remote area near a medical facility: Slowly rewarm by soaking in water no warmer than 105°F (40.5°C) until normal colour returns and the part feels warm
7. Loosely bandage with a dry sterile dressing
8. Fingers or toes affected: Place cotton or gauze between each digit
9. Do not break any blisters

## Immersion Foot (Trench Foot)

Immersion foot occurs when feet are exposed to cold, wet conditions for more than 12 hours. In a person with prior history or compromised foot health, it can develop in as little as 3 hours. Tissues do not freeze, but nerve and circulatory damage occurs.

### Signs and Symptoms of Immersion Foot — Initial (While Cold)

- Swollen, cold, waxy feet
- Peeling skin
- Reduced sensitivity to touch
- Wooden feeling in the feet
- Delayed capillary refill — nail beds remain white after pressure is released

### Signs and Symptoms of Immersion Foot — After Rewarming

- Warm, dry, red skin
- Pain
- Increased sensitivity to cold
- Blisters
- Tingling or itching

### Care and Management for Immersion Foot

1. Apply warm packs or soak feet in warm water (102°–105°F / 39°–40.5°C) for approximately 5 minutes
2. Elevate the affected area
3. Clean and dry the feet thoroughly
4. Give the person plenty of fluids to drink
5. Do not allow the person to wear wet socks or shoes
6. Obtain medical assistance as soon as possible

## Confined Space Emergencies

A confined space is any space with limited access that is not intended for continuous human occupancy. In wilderness and remote settings, confined spaces include crevasses, caves, abandoned mines, wells, culverts, and cisterns.

### Scene Safety Assessment for Confined Space Rescue

Do not enter a confined space if any of the following hazards are present:
- Fire
- Smoke-filled space
- Poison gas or hazardous material (HAZMAT) markings
- Risk of natural gas or propane explosion
- Collapsed or partially collapsed structure
- No ventilation or fresh air

Do not enter any confined space that requires special training or equipment (e.g., respirators, self-contained breathing apparatus [SCBA]).

### Actions for Confined Space Emergency Response

1. From outside the space: Determine how many people are involved and identify any hazards present
2. Call for help immediately — be as specific as possible about the situation so appropriate personnel are dispatched
3. Establish a safe perimeter — prevent anyone from entering the space
4. Do not enter unless the scene is confirmed safe
5. When safe: Assist in rescue and provide medical care as appropriate

**Note:** Do not use oxygen combined with an AED inside a confined space.
