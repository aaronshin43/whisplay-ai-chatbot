# Burns — Wilderness First Aid Reference

**Applies to:** All ages; field settings without rapid EMS access

---

## Overview of Burn Classification

Burns are caused by heat, chemical reactions, electricity (including lightning), and radiation (including sunburn). Severity is determined by two factors:
- **Depth:** Superficial, partial-thickness, or full-thickness
- **Extent:** Percentage of total body surface area (TBSA) affected

---

## Burn Depth Classification and Signs

### Superficial Burn
- Red, painful, and possibly swollen skin
- No blisters

### Partial-Thickness Burn
- Red, painful, and swollen skin
- Blisters — may not form until more than an hour after cooling

### Full-Thickness Burn
- Painless skin — nerve endings are destroyed
- Pale skin (scalding injury)
- Charred skin (fire injury)

---

## Burn Extent Assessment

- Use the **Rule of Palmar Surface:** The patient's own palm and fingers (inner surface) = 1% TBSA
- Greater TBSA burned = greater severity of injury

---

## Airway Complication Indicators in Burns

Suspect airway involvement and monitor closely for any of the following:
- Burns to the face and/or neck
- Soot in the mouth or nose
- Singed facial hair
- Dry cough

**Electrical burns** (lightning or electrocution): May affect cardiac and respiratory systems — always manage cardiac and respiratory problems before treating the burn itself.

---

## Care and Management for Burns

1. Remove the person from the burn source and immediate danger
2. Stop the burning process within 30 seconds if possible:
   - Remove the heat source
   - Smother any flames
   - Do not attempt to remove tar, melted plastic, or materials stuck to the burn
   - Electrical burn: Turn off electricity before approaching the person
   - Dry chemical burn: Brush off the chemical or remove contaminated clothing, then flood with water — take precautions against inhaling dust; prevent chemical from washing onto unaffected body parts
3. Manage airway, breathing, circulation; protect from shock and hypothermia
4. Cool the burn with cold running water for at least 20 minutes
5. Leave burn blisters intact — do not rupture
6. Remove jewellery from the burned area if possible — prevents complications from swelling
7. Apply a thin layer of wound gel to the burn surface
   - Wound gel or dressings unavailable: Leave the burn alone — the surface will dry into a protective scab-like covering
8. Cover with a gauze pad, thin layer of roll gauze, or clean clothing
9. **Do not apply ice** — ice causes frostbite and worsens tissue damage; do not pack burns in ice
10. Elevate burned extremities to minimise swelling
11. Encourage the person to gently and regularly move burned areas as tolerated
12. Keep the person well hydrated
13. Monitor the burn as an open wound

### Burn Re-Dressing Protocol (Twice Daily)

1. Soak off old dressings with clean, tepid water if needed
2. Rewash the wound to remove old wound gel
3. Apply a clean covering
4. Evacuation imminent within hours to one day: Do not re-dress — re-dressing is painful and unnecessary if evacuation is close

---

## Evacuation Criteria for Burns

Evacuate rapidly (GO FAST) for any of the following:
- Trouble breathing (suspected airway burn)
- Partial- or full-thickness burn covering ≥ 10% TBSA
- Partial- or full-thickness burn that is circumferential (wraps entirely around a body part, hand, foot, or other part)
- Serious burns of the head, face, neck, hands, feet, or genitals
- Full-thickness burn covering > 5% TBSA
- Burns caused by chemicals, explosions, or electricity
- Any partial- or full-thickness burn in a child under approximately age 5 or an adult over approximately age 60
