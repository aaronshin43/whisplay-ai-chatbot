# Approach to Shock — WHO BEC Reference

---

## Overview of Shock

Shock is the failure to deliver enough oxygen-carrying blood to the vital organs — this is called poor perfusion. Shock can lead rapidly to death. Infants, children, and older adults are at highest risk.

**Causes of poor perfusion leading to shock:**
- Blood loss (haemorrhage)
- Fluid loss from diarrhoea, vomiting, extensive burns, or excess urination (e.g., high blood sugar)
- Inadequate fluid intake — small children, the elderly, and the very ill cannot self-hydrate
- Abnormal blood vessel dilation (same blood volume, larger vessel space) — severe infection, spinal cord injury, severe allergic reaction
- Impaired cardiac filling — pericardial tamponade, tension pneumothorax
- Heart muscle pump failure — heart attack, myocarditis, arrhythmia, valve disease (cardiogenic shock)

**Initial assessment goal:** Identify shock and all reversible causes.

**Acute management goal:** Restore perfusion; address ongoing fluid loss where possible.

Always perform ABCDE first, then SAMPLE history, then secondary examination.

---

## ABCDE Key Elements in Shock

### A — Airway in Shock

- Face or mouth swelling and voice changes = possible anaphylaxis — severe allergic reaction can cause shock

### B — Breathing in Shock

- Wheeze = possible anaphylaxis causing shock
- Shock + DIB + absent breath sounds on one side = tension pneumothorax
- Poor perfusion itself drives rapid breathing when organs are not receiving enough oxygen
- Severe heart failure causes poor perfusion with DIB when fluid backs up into the lungs
- Any infection severe enough to cause shock may produce lung inflammation with DIB

### C — Circulation in Shock

- Shock can result from: gastrointestinal bleeding, pregnancy-related bleeding, internal and external traumatic haemorrhage, diarrhoea, vomiting, extensive burns, or excessive urination

### D — Disability in Shock

- Confusion with poor perfusion = severe shock
- Paralysis = possible spinal cord injury causing neurogenic shock

### E — Exposure in Shock

- Look for signs of bleeding, trauma, and diaphoresis
- Hives = allergic reaction; other rashes = systemic infection

## SAMPLE History for Shock

### S — Signs and Symptoms

- Vomiting or diarrhoea — ask frequency and duration: severe fluid loss can cause shock rapidly; large volumes of watery "rice-water" stool = suspect cholera
- Blood in stool or vomit (may appear black): Significant gastrointestinal bleeding can cause shock before it is recognised
- Vaginal bleeding: In women of childbearing age — always ask about pregnancy status, last menstrual period, missed periods, or recent pregnancy; ectopic pregnancy rupture can cause life-threatening shock before the woman knows she is pregnant
- Chest pain: Suspect heart attack — myocardial damage reduces pump function and causes cardiogenic shock
- Fever: Suspect severe infection causing septic shock — vasodilation and fluid leakage from vessels both reduce perfusion
- Exposure to toxins, medications, insect stings, or other substances: Severe allergic reaction causes shock; blood pressure and seizure medications can cause shock; anticoagulants worsen bleeding
- Altered mental status or unusual sleepiness: The brain is among the last organs affected by poor perfusion — altered mental status indicates severe shock

### A — Allergies

- Known allergies: Allergic reactions cause shock through abnormal blood vessel dilation

### M — Medications

- Full medication list: Heart medications may explain cardiogenic shock; blood pressure and seizure medications can cause or worsen shock; anticoagulants worsen bleeding; new medications or dose changes may explain allergic reaction

### P — Past Medical History

- Pregnancy, recent miscarriage, or recent delivery: Blood loss after delivery can be severe; ectopic pregnancy rupture can cause hidden haemorrhagic shock; all women of childbearing age with shock should be evaluated for pregnancy
- Recent surgery or induced abortion: Risk of internal bleeding or post-operative infection causing shock
- Heart disease (heart attack or valve problems): Risk of worsening heart function leading to or worsening shock
- HIV: Increases infection risk

### L — Last Oral Intake

- Poor oral intake can cause severe dehydration leading to shock — ask about recent eating and drinking

### E — Events Surrounding Illness

- Recent trauma: Risk of hidden internal bleeding, tension pneumothorax, pericardial haemorrhage, and spinal cord injury causing neurogenic shock
- Recent illness: Any infection can spread to the blood and cause septic shock

## Secondary Examination for Shock

A patient in shock will have some or all of: tachycardia, low systolic BP, tachypnoea, pale and cool skin, slow capillary refill, dizziness, confusion, decreased urine output, or diaphoresis.

Perfusion can be compromised before blood pressure falls — especially in the young. Low blood pressure with poor perfusion is a very serious sign. Always return to ABCDE if a primary survey problem is found during secondary examination.

### Breathing and Respiratory Rate

- Abnormal or noisy breathing = possible pneumonia as the source of systemic infection causing shock
- Deep or fast breathing + sweet or fruity breath = suspect DKA — elevated glucose causes excess urination, dehydration, and shock

### Bleeding Assessment

- Control all external bleeding with direct pressure immediately
- Arterial bleeding: Pulsing or high-pressure flow — significant blood volume can be lost within minutes
- Vaginal bleeding: Possible pregnancy-related haemorrhage even in women who do not know they are pregnant; possible cervical or uterine mass

### Fluid Status Assessment

- **Dehydration signs:** Thirst, dry lips and mouth, abnormal skin pinch, lethargy, delayed capillary refill, small amounts of dark urine
- **Volume overload signs (heart failure):** Bilateral leg swelling, lung crackles, distended neck veins — these patients are in shock but have excess fluid; treat cautiously

### Conjunctiva Assessment

- Pale inner lower eyelid (conjunctiva) = significant blood loss — compare to a healthy person or your own

### Mental Status

- Confusion with signs of poor perfusion = severe shock

### Fever

- Fever with shock = suspect severe infection

### Blood Glucose

- Low blood glucose can mimic shock
- Unable to check glucose but patient has altered mental status AND history of diabetes, malaria, or severe illness (especially children): Give glucose

### Abdomen

- Severe abdominal pain or a very firm abdomen = bleeding or infection in the abdomen; in a woman of childbearing age = suspect ectopic pregnancy

### Urine

- Small amounts of dark urine = substantial dehydration

### Stool

- Significant diarrhoea = dehydration risk; large amounts of watery rice-water stool = suspect cholera (report to public health)
- Black, dark, or reddish stool = gastrointestinal bleeding

### Skin, Swelling, and Rash

- Mouth or body swelling = allergic reaction
- Hives = allergic reaction; other rashes = systemic infection
- Bilateral leg swelling = heart failure
- Diaphoresis = moderate to severe shock

### Malnourishment

- Malnourished patients require adjusted fluid protocols — identify before starting fluids

## Causes of Shock — Signs by Category

### Poor Perfusion Due to Dilated Blood Vessels

- **Severe infection (septic shock):** Fever; tachycardia; tachypnoea; possible hypotension; possible visible infection source (skin, lung, urinary, focal pain with fever)
- **Spinal cord injury (neurogenic shock):** History or signs of trauma; spinal pain, tenderness, or vertebral malalignment; paralysis or weakness; tingling or loss of sensation; loss of bladder or bowel control; priapism; possible hypotension or bradycardia; DIB with upper cervical injury
- **Severe allergic reaction (anaphylactic shock):** Mouth swelling; DIB with stridor and/or wheeze; skin rash or hives; tachycardia; hypotension

### Poor Perfusion Due to Fluid Loss

- **Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA):** Known or new diabetes history; deep or rapid breathing; frequent urination; sweet-smelling breath; high blood or urine glucose; dehydration
- **Severe dehydration:** Abnormal skin pinch; decreased fluid intake or increased fluid loss (vomiting, diarrhoea, excess urination); dry mucous membranes; tachycardia
- **Burn injury:** Red, white, or black skin areas depending on depth; possible blistering; possible inhalation injury signs

### Poor Perfusion Due to Blood Loss

- **External bleeding:** History of trauma; visible bleeding; anticoagulant use
- **Large bone fracture (pelvic or femur):** Pelvic fracture — pelvic pain, blood at opening of penis or rectum; femur fracture — deformity or crepitus, leg shortening
- **Abdominal bleeding:** Bruising around umbilicus or flanks; abdominal pain; firm abdomen
- **Gastrointestinal bleeding:** Blood in vomit or stool; black vomit or stool; history of alcohol use
- **Haemothorax:** DIB; decreased breath sounds on affected side; dull percussion; shock if large volume
- **Ectopic pregnancy:** History of pregnancy or missed menstrual cycle, or any woman of childbearing age; abdominal pain; vaginal bleeding
- **Postpartum haemorrhage:** Recent delivery; heavy vaginal bleeding — pad or cloth soaked in < 5 minutes, constant trickling, bleeding > 250 mL, or soft uterus

### Poor Perfusion Due to Cardiac Problems

- **Heart failure:** DIB with exertion or when lying flat; bilateral leg swelling; distended neck veins; lung crackles; possible chest pain
- **Heart attack:** Pressure, tightness, or crushing chest sensation; diaphoresis; mottled skin; DIB; nausea or vomiting; jaw or arm pain; signs of heart failure; cardiac risk factors
- **Abnormal heart rhythm:** Very fast, very slow, or irregular pulse
- **Heart valve problem:** History of rheumatic fever or heart disease; heart murmur
- **Pericardial tamponade:** Signs of poor perfusion (tachycardia, tachypnoea, hypotension, pale and cold skin, capillary refill > 3 seconds); distended neck veins; muffled heart sounds; altered mental status; history of TB, trauma, cancer, or kidney failure
- **Tension pneumothorax:** Hypotension WITH DIB AND absent breath sounds on affected side AND hyperresonance on percussion AND distended neck veins AND possible tracheal shift

**Note:** Hypoglycaemia can mimic shock — signs include diaphoresis, seizure, blood glucose < 3.5 mmol/L, altered mental status, and history of diabetes, malaria, or severe illness (especially in children).

## General Shock Management

Perform ABCDE first and intervene for all life-threatening conditions. All shock patients require oxygen.

**Position:** Keep the patient flat (supine). Do not sit the patient up unless breathing is severely compromised (heart failure, pulmonary oedema).

### IV Fluid Protocol for Shock (Normal Nutritional Status)

**Caution: Do not use standard fluid protocol for severely malnourished patients, severely anaemic patients, or anyone with signs of volume overload — use adjusted protocol.**

1. Establish two large-bore IV lines immediately
2. Start IV fluids — isotonic crystalloid: normal saline or Ringer's Lactate (adults and children with normal nutritional status)
3. Reassess perfusion immediately after each bolus
4. Perfusion improves: Continue IV fluids
5. Perfusion does not improve: Re-bolus immediately
6. IV access not achievable: Call immediately for a provider who can place a nasogastric (NG) tube or intraosseous (IO) line; offer oral fluids if safe to do so in the meantime

IV fluids address only immediate perfusion. All patients in shock need transfer to a unit capable of treating the underlying cause, including transfusion.

## Condition-Specific Shock Management

### Burns
- Burns cause major fluid losses through disrupted skin — calculate replacement using burn size; these patients have different fluid needs from standard shock patients

### Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)
1. Give IV fluids
2. Arrange urgent transfer to a unit with IV infusion and close monitoring — DKA is a critical emergency

### Severe Infection (Septic Shock)
1. Give IV fluids
2. Start antibiotics immediately
3. Suspected infectious diarrhoea (e.g., cholera): Use gloves, aprons, and relevant isolation precautions; report to local public health authorities
4. Signs of poor perfusion not improving with fluids: Arrange rapid transfer

### Spinal Cord Injury (Neurogenic Shock)
1. Give IV fluids
2. Arrange transfer to a unit capable of providing spinal care

### Gastrointestinal Bleeding
1. Start IV fluids
2. Arrange transfer for blood transfusion

### Ectopic Pregnancy
1. Give IV fluids
2. Arrange transfer for blood transfusion and obstetric care immediately

### Postpartum Haemorrhage

1. Arrange immediate transfer to a qualified obstetric provider — all patients require this
2. Attempt to control bleeding while arranging and during transfer:
   - Perform uterine massage until the uterus is hard (firm on palpation)
   - Give intramuscular (IM) oxytocin — this is a loading dose
   - Give IV fluids and IV oxytocin concurrently
   - Empty the bladder
3. Placenta not yet delivered: Continue uterine massage — when uterus is firm, the placenta will typically deliver
4. Placenta delivered: Collect in a leak-proof container and send with the patient for inspection by the receiving provider; continue oxytocin
5. Perineal or vaginal tear present: Apply direct pressure with sterile gauze; put legs together
6. Still bleeding: Continue IV fluids with oxytocin; insert a second IV line
7. Continue oxytocin for at least 1 hour after bleeding stops
8. Even if bleeding stops: Transfer immediately — these patients still require advanced obstetric care

### Tension Pneumothorax
1. Perform needle decompression immediately
2. Give oxygen and IV fluids
3. Arrange urgent transfer for chest tube placement

### Pericardial Tamponade
1. Give IV fluids to maintain cardiac filling against the building pericardial pressure
2. Arrange urgent transfer for pericardiocentesis (drainage of pericardial fluid)

### Suspected Heart Attack
1. Give aspirin if indicated
2. Establish IV access; give IV fluids; reassess frequently
3. Give oxygen if shock or DIB is present — oxygen is not required for all heart attack patients
4. Arrange rapid transfer to an advanced provider

### Heart Failure
1. Give IV fluids slowly
2. Check lungs for crackles every 5 minutes — stop IV fluids if fluid overload develops (DIB, lung crackles, increased RR, increased HR)
3. Arrange rapid transfer to an advanced provider

### Severe Allergic Reaction (Anaphylactic Shock)
1. Give intramuscular adrenaline immediately
2. Establish IV access and give IV fluids — condition can worsen rapidly when adrenaline wears off
3. Give a second adrenaline dose if effects wear off and symptoms persist
4. Airway swelling or DIB present: Arrange rapid transfer for advanced airway management

### Traumatic Haemorrhagic Shock
1. Stop all bleeding using direct pressure, wound packing, tourniquet, pelvic binder, or fracture splinting as appropriate
2. Give IV fluids
3. Perform a thorough trauma assessment
4. Arrange transfer for blood transfusion and surgical management

## Paediatric Shock — Special Considerations

Shock develops rapidly in children and is life-threatening. Children have a larger surface area relative to body volume — they dehydrate faster. Infants and young children cannot communicate thirst and cannot self-hydrate.

### Clinical Definition of Shock in Children (WHO 2016)

All three of the following:
- Cold extremities
- Capillary refill > 3 seconds
- Weak and fast pulse

Additional signs of poor perfusion: Low blood pressure, fast breathing, altered mental status, decreased urination (ask parents about nappy frequency).

### Signs of Dehydration in Children

- Very dry mouth and lips
- Lethargy (excessive drowsiness, slow to respond, not interactive)
- Sunken eyes
- Small amounts of dark urine
- Sunken fontanelle in infants under 1 year
- Delayed capillary refill (normal is < 3 seconds)
- Abnormal skin pinch
- Pallor — anaemia makes dehydration harder to treat

### Common Causes of Shock and Dehydration in Children

- **Vomiting and diarrhoea (gastroenteritis):** Sudden onset vomiting and diarrhoea with abdominal pain and fever; large amounts of watery diarrhoea = suspect cholera (report to public health)
- **Vomiting without diarrhoea:** Suspect raised intracranial pressure (trauma, tumour, brain swelling) or intestinal blockage — examine for trauma; vomiting with fever = suspect infection
- **Overwhelming infection:** Fever causes rapid fluid loss; severe infection also causes vasodilation, worsening shock

### Management of Shock in Malnourished Children

- Malnourished children are at high risk for hypoglycaemia — give sugar in addition to fluids
- Give specialised fluids if available
- Give less IV fluid at a slower rate — check lungs for crackles every 5 minutes
- Stop IV fluids immediately if fluid overload develops (crackles, increased RR, increased HR)
- Switch to oral fluids as soon as signs of poor perfusion improve
- Arrange rapid transfer to a unit with blood transfusion capabilities

## Disposition Considerations for Shock

- Patients in shock can deteriorate and die very quickly — monitor continuously
- The same conditions causing shock impair fluid regulation — monitor closely for signs of DIB throughout
- Confused or anxious patients in shock: Ensure safety and containment during transfer
- Patients requiring transfusion or surgery: Communicate directly with the receiving facility to confirm blood products and surgical capacity are available before transfer
