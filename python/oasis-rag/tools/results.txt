## Bleeding Control
**Types:** Arterial (spurting, life-threatening), Venous (smooth flow), Internal (bruising). 
**Management Steps:**
1. **Direct Pressure:** Apply firm pressure directly to the source using gauze or a clean pad (2-3 fingers). If blood-soaked, add another pad on top; do not remove the original.
2. **Deep Wound Packing:** If simple pressure fails on a deep/gaping wound, pack the wound completely with clean material/gauze, then apply pressure over it. *Do not leave packing >24 hours.*
3. **Tourniquet Application:** Use ONLY if other measures fail and bleeding is life-threatening. Apply on extremities only, above the bleeding site (prefer upper arm/leg), using a wide band. Tighten until bleeding stops. **Record exact application time.** *Never loosen a tourniquet in the pre-hospital setting.*

## Initiation Criteria
Start CPR IMMEDIATELY if the patient is:
- Unresponsive (does not react to tapping shoulders and shouting)
- Not breathing normally (or only gasping)
- Lacks a clear pulse

*Delaying CPR reduces survival by 7-10% per minute.*

### Adult CPR Protocol
1. **Scene Safety:** Ensure the area is safe.
2. **Call for Help:** Call emergency services and explicitly delegate a bystander to get an AED.
3. **Open Airway:** Use head-tilt/chin-lift (if suspected spine injury, use jaw-thrust ONLY).
4. **Check Breathing:** Look, listen, and feel for normal breathing for NO MORE than 10 seconds. Gasping is abnormal—begin CPR if found.
5. **Chest Compressions:**
   - Kneel beside caller. Place heel of one hand on center of lower half of sternum, second hand interlocked on top.
   - Posture: Arms perfectly straight, shoulders directly over hands.
   - Depth: Push down firmly 5 to 6 cm (2 to 2.5 inches).
   - Rate: 100 to 120 compressions per minute.
   - Recoil: Allow full chest recoil between compressions. Do NOT lean on the chest.
6. **Rescue Breaths:**
   - Pinch nose, make an airtight seal over mouth.
   - Blow for exactly 1 second, watch for chest rise. Remove mouth, let chest fall, deliver second breath.
   - If chest doesn't rise, intuitively reposition the head and retry ONE time.
7. **CPR Cycle:** **30 Compressions followed by 2 Rescue Breaths.** Do not pause.
8. **Rescuer Fatigue:** Switch active compressor every 2 minutes. If you are untrained or unwilling to give breaths, deliver *compression-only CPR*.

- Unconscious patient, not breathing normally, no trauma: Open airway with head-tilt chin-lift

## Heat Exhaustion

Heat exhaustion results from heat stress combined with water and electrolyte loss (primarily through sweat) and inadequate hydration, typically during exercise.

### Signs and Symptoms of Heat Exhaustion

- Sweating
- Exhaustion
- Pale, sweaty, or flushed skin
- Headache
- Nausea and/or vomiting
- Thirst and decreased urine output — urine is yellowish
- Dizziness on standing quickly (orthostatic)
- Elevated heart rate and respiratory rate
- Slightly elevated core temperature (not always present)

### Care and Management for Heat Exhaustion

1. Stop all activity; have the person rest in a cool, shady place
2. Replace lost fluids and salt — give small amounts of carbohydrate-electrolyte solution (sports drink or fruit juice) frequently; do not give salt tablets (too concentrated)
3. Loosen or remove as much clothing as possible
4. Wet down the skin and fan the person continuously to increase evaporative cooling
5. Person drowsy: Allow to sleep
6. Person feels better: May resume normal activity
7. Monitor for recurrence — recovery may take up to 24 hours

**Critical distinction from hyponatremia:** Heat exhaustion produces yellowish urine and thirst. Hyponatremia produces clear urine with no thirst — do not treat hyponatremia with water (see Hyponatremia section).

## General Shock Management

Perform ABCDE first and intervene for all life-threatening conditions. All shock patients require oxygen.

**Position:** Keep the patient flat (supine). Do not sit the patient up unless breathing is severely compromised (heart failure, pulmonary oedema).

### IV Fluid Protocol for Shock (Normal Nutritional Status)

**Caution: Do not use standard fluid protocol for severely malnourished patients, severely anaemic patients, or anyone with signs of volume overload — use adjusted protocol.**

1. Establish two large-bore IV lines immediately
2. Start IV fluids — isotonic crystalloid: normal saline or Ringer's Lactate (adults and children with normal nutritional status)
3. Reassess perfusion immediately after each bolus
4. Perfusion improves: Continue IV fluids
5. Perfusion does not improve: Re-bolus immediately
6. IV access not achievable: Call immediately for a provider who can place a nasogastric (NG) tube or intraosseous (IO) line; offer oral fluids if safe to do so in the meantime

IV fluids address only immediate perfusion. All patients in shock need transfer to a unit capable of treating the underlying cause, including transfusion.

**Depth Classification**
- **Superficial (First-Degree):** Red, painful, intact skin, no blisters. Quick capillary refill.
- **Partial-Thickness (Second-Degree):** Red/mottled, painful, blisters, wet. Temporary whitening on pressure.

**Depth Classification**

- **Partial-Thickness (Second-Degree):** Red/mottled, painful, blisters, wet. Temporary whitening on pressure.

### Assessment After Submersion

Assess in order: unconsciousness → airway → breathing → circulation → disability → environmental conditions

### Care and Management for Near-Drowning

1. Unconscious and not breathing: Give 2 rescue breaths
   - Chest rises clearly: Begin CPR immediately
   - Chest does not rise on first breath: Retilt the head before giving the second breath
   - Chest still does not rise: Airway may be blocked — after each subsequent set of chest compressions, look for an obstruction before attempting breaths; remove if visible; continue CPR
2. Do not attempt to clear the lungs of water — this is ineffective and wastes time
3. Be ready to roll the person onto their side to clear the airway if water or vomit comes up
4. Suspected head, neck, or back injury: Immobilise the spine before and during movement
5. Treat concurrently for shock, hypothermia, or any underlying condition that may have caused the person's distress in the water (e.g., diabetic emergency)

**SCUBA diving incidents:** Risks from compressed air breathing may require transport to a hyperbaric chamber — arrange advanced care immediately.

## Insect & Arthropod Bites
- **Tick Bites:** Remove immediately by grasping close to skin with tweezers and pulling slowly. Wash site. If flu-like symptoms occur within 2 weeks, seek medical evaluation.
- **Bee/Wasp Stings:** Scrape away stinger or use tweezers (don't squeeze venom sac). Wash site, apply cold pack. Monitor for anaphylaxis.
- **Spider (Black Widow/Brown Recluse) & Scorpion:** Wash, apply wound gel/bandage, apply cold pack. Use antivenin if available.

## Animal & Marine Bites/Stings
- **Animal Bites:** Control serious bleeding first. Wash minor wounds with soap/water and irrigate heavily. Leave serious wounds for a medical facility. Monitor for infection (rabies risk).
- **Jellyfish Sting:** Irrigate with vinegar for at least 30 seconds (or baking soda slurry). After stinging stops, immerse in hot water. *Portuguese Man-of-War:* Use ocean water, NOT vinegar.
- **Stingray:** Flush with water, soak in non-scalding hot water until pain resolves. Immobilize.
